# Chapter 12: Learner Identity, Agency, and AI

## Introduction: The Social Self in Algorithmic Spaces

The preceding chapters have examined AI's relationship to the cognitive and linguistic dimensions of second language acquisition: input, interaction, output, feedback, pronunciation, and assessment. These dimensions, while essential, tell only part of the story. Language learning is not merely a cognitive process of internalizing grammatical rules and lexical items; it is a profoundly social act--an investment of the self in new ways of being, belonging, and making meaning in the world (Norton, 2013). To learn a second language is to negotiate identity: to decide who one is, who one wishes to become, and how language mediates access to the communities and resources that matter to the learner. Any technology that intervenes in the language learning process also intervenes in these identity negotiations, whether its designers intend this or not.

Artificial intelligence intervenes in language learner identity in ways that are both novel and undertheorized. When a learner converses with an AI chatbot in their L2, they are engaging in a form of social interaction that is--and is not--like talking to a human. The social risks of face-to-face L2 use (embarrassment, misunderstanding, judgment, exclusion) are attenuated; the learner can stumble, restart, and experiment without fear of social consequences. This attenuation of social risk is marketed as a benefit, and for many learners it genuinely is. But what is lost when the social risk is removed? If language learning is, as Norton (2013) argues, fundamentally about gaining access to social networks, symbolic resources, and imagined communities, what happens when the "community" one practices with is an algorithm that has no social existence, no cultural membership, no power to grant or deny access to anything?

This chapter takes up these questions through the theoretical lenses of identity, investment, and agency as they have been developed in critical applied linguistics. It begins with Norton's foundational theory of investment and its extension by Darvin and Norton into digital spaces. It then examines how AI shapes, constrains, and potentially transforms learner identity construction. The chapter addresses learner agency and autonomy in AI-mediated environments, interrogates the digital literacy competencies required for effective AI-mediated L2 learning, and engages with critical and decolonial perspectives on AI in language education. It concludes with an examination of how learners actually use--and resist, subvert, and appropriate--AI tools in ways that exceed and challenge designer intentions.

## 12.1 Norton's Investment Theory and Imagined Communities

### 12.1.1 From Motivation to Investment

Bonny Norton's reconceptualization of language learning motivation as "investment" represents one of the most significant theoretical contributions to SLA in the past three decades (Norton Peirce, 1995; Norton, 2000, 2013). Drawing on Bourdieu's (1977, 1991) concepts of cultural capital and symbolic power, Norton argued that the concept of "motivation"--as used in mainstream SLA research influenced by Gardner and Lambert (1972)--failed to account for the social and power-related dimensions of language learning. A learner might be highly "motivated" in the psychometric sense (high integrative and instrumental motivation) and yet not invest in language learning because the social conditions--discrimination, marginalization, lack of access to target language communities--made investment irrational or impossible.

Norton proposed "investment" as an alternative construct that captures the relationship between the language learner's identity and their commitment to learning. Learners invest in a second language when they understand that learning the language will increase their cultural capital and social power--that it will give them access to resources, relationships, and identities that are denied to them without the language. Investment is not a stable trait of the learner but a dynamic, situated response to social conditions that either reward or punish the learner's efforts to participate in target language communities.

This theoretical reorientation has direct implications for AI-mediated language learning. When a learner practices speaking with an AI tutor, what are they investing in? The learner's investment is presumably oriented toward future interactions with human interlocutors--toward gaining the linguistic capital needed to participate in target language communities. But the AI interaction itself does not grant access to any community; it does not increase the learner's social network, cultural capital, or symbolic power. It is practice for future investment rather than investment itself. The question is whether this practice-without-stakes is sufficient to sustain the kind of committed, identity-shaping engagement that Norton identifies as the engine of acquisition, or whether the removal of social stakes also removes the social rewards that make investment worthwhile.

### 12.1.2 Imagined Communities and AI

Norton's concept of "imagined communities" (Norton, 2001; Kanno & Norton, 2003), drawing on Anderson's (1991) work, refers to the communities of practice that learners aspire to join but have not yet accessed. A Japanese student learning English may imagine herself as a member of an international academic community; a Syrian refugee in Germany may imagine himself as a participating citizen of German society; a heritage Spanish speaker in the United States may imagine reconnecting with family and cultural roots through the language. These imagined communities shape investment by providing a vision of the future self that makes present effort meaningful.

AI language learning tools interact with imagined communities in complex ways. On one hand, AI can bring imagined communities closer: an AI tutor that simulates academic English discourse gives the Japanese student a foretaste of her imagined community; an AI system that generates realistic German bureaucratic dialogues prepares the Syrian refugee for interactions he will face. On the other hand, AI can substitute for imagined communities in ways that may be counterproductive: if the learner's primary L2 interaction is with AI, the imagined community may become the AI itself--an impoverished community of one algorithm that cannot grant membership, status, or belonging.

The risk is particularly acute for learners who lack access to target language speakers. For an English learner in a rural area of Vietnam, an AI English tutor may be the only available interlocutor. If this learner comes to experience AI interaction as the primary site of English use, rather than as preparation for future human interaction, the imagined community may shrink from "the global English-speaking world" to "me and my phone." The motivational and identity-forming potential of imagined communities depends on their social richness--on the learner's ability to envision themselves in webs of human relationship--and AI interaction, however linguistically sophisticated, does not provide that social richness.

### 12.1.3 Investment in the Digital Age

Norton and colleagues have increasingly attended to how digital spaces--social media, online gaming, digital storytelling, and now AI--create new sites for identity construction and investment (Darvin & Norton, 2015; Norton & Toohey, 2011). Digital spaces can expand access to target language communities that are geographically inaccessible, provide safe spaces for identity experimentation, and enable learners to project desired identities through curated digital performances. A language learner can join a Discord server in their L2, participate in Reddit communities, create TikTok content, or engage in online multiplayer games, each providing authentic communicative contexts in which identity is constructed through language use.

AI tools occupy an ambiguous position within this digital landscape. They are digital, but they are not social in the way that online communities are social. They can simulate interaction, but they cannot offer the genuine human recognition--the sense of being seen, heard, and valued by another consciousness--that drives investment. When a learner receives encouragement from an AI tutor ("Great job! Your pronunciation is improving!"), this feedback may be functionally useful but is ontologically different from encouragement received from a human teacher who knows the learner's struggles, cares about their progress, and has a stake in the relationship. The question of whether AI feedback can sustain investment in the same way that human recognition can is not merely philosophical; it has practical implications for how AI tools are positioned in language learning programs.

## 12.2 Darvin and Norton's Model of Investment in Digital Spaces

### 12.2.1 The Ideological Framework

Darvin and Norton (2015) extended Norton's investment theory to address the digital age through a model that positions investment at the intersection of three constructs: identity, ideology, and capital. In this model, learner investment is shaped not only by the individual's identity and desires but by the ideological structures that determine what counts as legitimate language, who counts as a legitimate speaker, and what forms of cultural capital are valued in a given context.

This ideological dimension is critical for understanding AI-mediated language learning. AI systems are not ideologically neutral; they embody the values, assumptions, and power structures of their creators and training data. An AI English tutor trained on Standard American English encodes an ideology about what "good English" sounds like. An AI writing assistant that penalizes informal register encodes an ideology about what "good writing" looks like. An AI assessment system that evaluates communicative competence against monolingual native speaker norms encodes an ideology about who counts as a competent speaker (see Chapters 10 and 11).

Darvin and Norton's model directs our attention to how these ideological encodings interact with learner identity. When a learner whose L1 is a non-prestige variety encounters an AI system that consistently "corrects" features of their speech or writing that are actually features of their variety, the system is performing an ideological act: it is telling the learner that their way of using language is deficient. This message may be internalized, shaping the learner's linguistic identity in ways that devalue their existing competence. Or it may be resisted, with the learner recognizing the system's norms as arbitrary and choosing to maintain their variety. The outcome depends on the learner's critical awareness, the availability of alternative frames of reference, and the power dynamics of the educational context.

### 12.2.2 Capital and AI-Mediated Language Learning

Bourdieu's (1991) concept of capital--economic, cultural, social, and symbolic--provides a framework for analyzing what learners gain (or fail to gain) from AI-mediated language learning. Language itself functions as cultural capital: proficiency in a prestige language or variety confers access to educational, professional, and social opportunities. Language learning is an investment of time and effort (economic capital) in the acquisition of linguistic capital, with the expectation that this linguistic capital will be convertible into other forms of capital (employment, social networks, citizenship).

AI language learning tools are positioned in the market as efficient mechanisms for acquiring linguistic capital. Their value proposition is fundamentally economic: they offer language learning at lower cost (compared to private tutoring or immersion programs), greater convenience (available anytime, anywhere), and higher efficiency (adaptive algorithms that optimize learning time). This framing aligns with the neoliberal logic that treats language learning as a human capital investment--a rational, individual choice to increase one's market value.

However, the capital that AI-mediated language learning produces may differ from the capital that human-mediated learning produces. AI interaction does not generate social capital (relationships with target language speakers, membership in communities of practice); it generates only linguistic capital, and that capital is limited to the forms of language that the AI system recognizes and rewards. A learner who achieves high scores on an AI pronunciation system but has never negotiated meaning with a human interlocutor possesses a specific type of linguistic capital that may or may not transfer to the social contexts where capital conversion occurs.

### 12.2.3 Digital Identities and AI

Darvin and Norton's model recognizes that digital spaces create new possibilities for identity construction. In online environments, learners can experiment with identities that may be unavailable to them in face-to-face contexts: a shy learner can be bold in a chatroom; a learner who is silenced by power dynamics in a classroom can have a voice in an online forum; a learner whose accent is stigmatized can present themselves through text.

AI interlocutors extend this identity experimentation in specific ways. Because AI systems do not judge, remember, or gossip, they create spaces where learners can try on L2 identities without social consequences. A learner can practice assertive language, experiment with humor in the L2, rehearse professional or academic registers, or explore identity positions (gendered language, formal/informal code-switching, cultural expression) that they might not risk in human interaction. This is a genuine affordance: the low-stakes identity laboratory that AI provides may be particularly valuable for learners who face stigma, discrimination, or anxiety in face-to-face L2 use.

The limitation is that identity constructed in AI interaction may be fragile precisely because it has not been tested in social reality. An identity that "works" with an AI tutor--that receives positive feedback and generates a sense of competence--may not survive contact with human interlocutors who respond unpredictably, who bring their own power dynamics and biases, and who may not validate the learner's self-presentation. The transfer of AI-constructed identity to human social contexts is an empirical question that the field has barely begun to investigate.

## 12.3 How AI Shapes and Constrains Learner Identity

### 12.3.1 The Standardization Effect

AI language learning systems, by their nature, tend toward standardization. They are trained on corpora that represent particular varieties, registers, and genres; they evaluate learner output against those standards; and they provide feedback that pushes learners toward conformity with the training norms. This standardization effect has implications for learner identity that extend beyond the purely linguistic.

When an AI system consistently rewards Standard English and penalizes non-standard features, it sends a message about which linguistic identities are valued and which are not. For heritage language learners, this can be particularly consequential. A U.S.-born heritage Spanish speaker whose home variety includes features of Chicano Spanish (code-switching, English loanwords, distinctive phonological patterns) may find that an AI Spanish tutor "corrects" features that are central to their linguistic identity and family belonging. The AI system, calibrated to a peninsular or "standard" Latin American variety, does not recognize the learner's variety as legitimate--and this non-recognition is an identity wound.

Kramsch (2009) has argued that language learners are "multilingual subjects" who construct identities at the intersection of multiple languages and cultures. AI systems that enforce monolingual, standard-language norms may constrain this multilingual identity construction by treating each language as a discrete, bounded system rather than as part of a fluid multilingual repertoire. The learner who naturally translanguages--drawing on resources from multiple languages to make meaning--encounters an AI system that insists on language separation, potentially undermining a communicative strategy that is both cognitively natural and identity-affirming.

### 12.3.2 The Persona Problem

AI language tutors are increasingly designed with "personas"--names, avatars, personality traits, and conversational styles that simulate human-like interaction. Duolingo's characters (Lily, Oscar, Zari, Bea, Junior) have distinct personalities and backstories. Replika offers AI companions with customizable relationships. Speak presents AI tutors with names and speaking styles. These personas are designed to increase engagement and create a sense of relational interaction.

The construction of AI personas raises identity-related questions. First, there is the question of representation: AI tutor personas overwhelmingly represent the target language culture (or a homogenized, globalized version of it), potentially reinforcing the association between language proficiency and cultural assimilation. A learner who interacts primarily with an AI persona named "Emma" who speaks Standard American English and references American cultural touchpoints may internalize the message that becoming proficient in English means becoming culturally American--a message that erases the possibility of bilingual, bicultural, or translingual identities.

Second, there is the question of what happens to the learner's sense of self when their primary L2 interlocutor is an AI persona. Block (2007) documented how L2 learners' identities are shaped by their interactions with specific individuals--teachers, friends, romantic partners--who serve as models, gatekeepers, and mirrors for the learner's developing L2 self. An AI persona cannot serve these functions in the same way, because it has no subjectivity, no genuine interest in the learner, and no capacity for the kind of reciprocal recognition that identity formation requires. The learner may develop a sense of competence in the AI interaction but not the sense of belonging that comes from being recognized as a legitimate participant by a human community.

### 12.3.3 The Infantilization Risk

Many AI language learning tools, particularly those designed for mobile consumption, employ gamification strategies--points, streaks, leaderboards, cartoon characters, celebratory animations--that may infantilize adult learners. The design idiom of these tools draws more from mobile gaming than from adult education, and the result can be an experience that positions the learner as a child to be entertained and managed rather than an adult with complex communicative goals, professional needs, and identity investments.

For adult immigrants, professionals, and graduate students--populations with high-stakes language learning needs--the infantilizing design of many AI language tools may conflict with their desired identity. An engineer who needs to present at international conferences is poorly served by an app that rewards correct answers with animated dancing owls. A refugee who needs to navigate bureaucratic systems is poorly served by a gamified grammar drill that treats language as a game rather than a survival tool. The mismatch between the tool's implied user (a casual, entertainment-oriented consumer) and the actual user (an adult with urgent, identity-laden language needs) represents a design failure that has identity consequences.

## 12.4 Agency and Autonomy in AI-Mediated Language Learning

### 12.4.1 Defining Agency in SLA

Agency--the capacity to act intentionally and make meaningful choices--has emerged as a central construct in sociocultural approaches to SLA (Ahearn, 2001; Duff, 2012; Lantolf & Thorne, 2006). In language learning contexts, agency manifests as the learner's capacity to set goals, choose learning strategies, resist or negotiate classroom power dynamics, and shape their own learning trajectory. Agency is not an individual trait but a socially situated capacity: it is enabled or constrained by the social, institutional, and material conditions in which the learner operates (van Lier, 2008).

The relationship between AI and learner agency is paradoxical. On one hand, AI tools can enhance agency by providing learners with resources and choices that were previously unavailable. A learner with access to an AI tutor can choose when, where, and how to practice; can direct the conversation toward topics of personal interest; can control the difficulty level of input; and can seek feedback on specific aspects of their language use. This self-directed, customizable learning environment represents an expansion of learner agency relative to the teacher-controlled classroom.

On the other hand, AI systems constrain agency in ways that may be less visible but equally consequential. The algorithms that determine what content a learner sees, what difficulty level they practice at, and what feedback they receive make choices on the learner's behalf, often without transparency about how those choices are made. The learner experiences a curated learning environment that feels autonomous but is, in fact, algorithmically managed. The distinction between genuine autonomy (the learner makes informed choices about their learning) and algorithmic autonomy (the system makes choices that the learner passively accepts) is critical and often blurred in AI learning platforms.

### 12.4.2 Algorithmic Determination vs. Learner Self-Direction

The tension between algorithmic determination and learner self-direction plays out concretely in the design of AI learning platforms. Adaptive learning systems, by definition, make decisions about what the learner should do next: which vocabulary to review, which grammar points to practice, which difficulty level to present. These decisions are based on algorithms that model the learner's current knowledge state and optimize for some objective function (typically, maximizing accuracy or minimizing time to proficiency).

The problem is that the algorithm's objective function may not align with the learner's goals, needs, or identity. A learner who wants to read poetry in Spanish will not benefit from an algorithm that optimizes for conversational fluency. A learner who is preparing for a specific professional context (medical, legal, technical) will not benefit from a general-purpose language curriculum. A learner who values cultural knowledge as much as linguistic accuracy will not benefit from a system that treats language as a decontextualized skill to be drilled.

Benson (2011) has argued that autonomy in language learning requires the learner to exercise control over three dimensions: learning management (when, where, and how to learn), cognitive processing (how to engage with input and production), and learning content (what to learn). Most AI language learning platforms allow learners significant control over learning management (they can practice anytime, anywhere) but limited control over cognitive processing (the system determines the practice format) and minimal control over learning content (the curriculum is algorithmically generated). This selective enhancement of autonomy--expanding freedom on one dimension while constraining it on others--creates a simulacrum of self-directed learning that may satisfy learners' desire for control without providing genuine agency over the content and process of their learning.

### 12.4.3 The Autonomy Paradox

There is a deeper paradox in the relationship between AI and learner autonomy. Autonomy, as conceived in language learning theory (Holec, 1981; Little, 1991; Benson, 2011), is not merely the freedom to make choices; it is the capacity to make informed, reflective choices about one's own learning. This capacity develops through experience, reflection, and metacognitive awareness. It is a learning outcome, not just a learning condition.

AI tools that make learning decisions on the learner's behalf may undermine the development of this capacity. If the algorithm decides what to practice, the learner does not develop the metacognitive skills needed to identify their own learning needs. If the algorithm provides immediate feedback, the learner does not develop the self-monitoring skills needed to evaluate their own production. If the algorithm sequences content optimally, the learner does not develop the planning skills needed to manage their own learning trajectory. The AI system, by being helpful, may prevent the learner from developing the autonomy that would make the system unnecessary.

This is the autonomy paradox of AI-mediated learning: the more effectively the system manages learning, the less the learner develops the capacity for self-management. The resolution of this paradox lies in design choices that deliberately foster metacognitive awareness--AI systems that explain their recommendations, invite learner input into content selection, prompt self-reflection, and gradually transfer decision-making authority from the algorithm to the learner. Such systems exist in prototype (e.g., the MASELTOV project for immigrant language learners; Krug et al., 2016) but are rare in commercial products, where the design incentive is to keep users engaged within the platform rather than to develop the self-directed learning skills that would enable them to leave it.

## 12.5 Digital Literacy as a Component of L2 Competence

### 12.5.1 What Digital Literacy Means for Language Learners

The integration of AI into language learning creates a new domain of competence that language learners must develop: digital literacy as it relates to L2 use. This is not simply the ability to use technology (operational literacy) but encompasses the critical ability to evaluate AI-generated content, to understand how AI systems process language, and to make informed decisions about when and how to use AI tools in L2 communication.

Kern (2015) has argued that literacy in the digital age involves not only the ability to read and write but the ability to navigate multimodal, intertextual, and technologically mediated communication environments. For L2 learners, this digital literacy has additional dimensions: the ability to distinguish between AI-generated and human-generated text in the L2, the ability to evaluate the accuracy and appropriateness of AI translations and suggestions, the ability to use AI tools as scaffolds without becoming dependent on them, and the critical awareness to recognize the linguistic norms and cultural biases embedded in AI systems.

### 12.5.2 AI Literacy as Critical Language Awareness

Critical Language Awareness (CLA), as developed by Fairclough (1992) and applied to L2 education by various scholars, involves understanding how language functions in social life--how it constructs and maintains power relations, how it is used to include and exclude, how standard language ideologies operate. In the age of AI, CLA must extend to encompass awareness of how AI systems mediate language use.

An AI-literate language learner would understand, for example, that when an AI translation tool produces a translation, the output reflects statistical patterns in the training data rather than a communicative interpretation of the source text. They would understand that when an AI writing assistant suggests a word choice, the suggestion is based on frequency in the training corpus rather than on what is most appropriate for the communicative context. They would understand that when an AI pronunciation system rates their speech, the rating reflects proximity to one particular variety rather than communicative effectiveness in general.

This critical awareness is not a luxury; it is a necessity for effective, autonomous L2 use in AI-mediated environments. Without it, learners may accept AI outputs uncritically, internalize AI norms without questioning them, and develop a form of learned helplessness in which they defer to AI judgments rather than developing their own communicative competence. Language teachers have a responsibility to develop this AI literacy alongside linguistic competence, treating it as an integral component of communicative language ability rather than a supplementary digital skill.

### 12.5.3 The Dependency Risk

The most frequently expressed concern about AI tools in language learning--and one with substantial empirical support--is the risk of learner dependency. When an AI writing assistant can correct grammar, suggest vocabulary, and improve coherence in real time, the learner may become reliant on these supports and fail to develop the independent production skills that constitute genuine proficiency. When an AI translation tool is always available, the learner may bypass the effortful processing (comprehension strategies, vocabulary inference, syntactic parsing) that SLA theory identifies as essential for acquisition.

The dependency risk is supported by research on the "generation effect" in cognitive psychology (Slamecka & Graf, 1978): information that learners generate themselves is better retained than information provided to them. AI tools that provide answers--correct spellings, appropriate vocabulary, grammatical corrections--may deprive learners of the generative processing that promotes retention. The parallel to Swain's (1985) Output Hypothesis is direct: pushed output--the struggle to produce language beyond one's current level--promotes noticing, hypothesis testing, and metalinguistic reflection. AI scaffolding that eliminates this struggle may improve immediate performance while undermining long-term acquisition.

The dependency risk is not unique to AI tools; it applies to any form of scaffolding, including human tutoring. The distinguishing factor is the always-available, frictionless nature of AI support. A human tutor is available for limited hours and may deliberately withhold support to promote learner independence; an AI tool is available 24/7 and is designed to provide maximum support, because user satisfaction (and continued subscription revenue) depends on making the learner feel helped rather than challenged.

## 12.6 The Neoliberal Critique: AI as Commodification of Language Learning

### 12.6.1 Language Learning as Market Transaction

Critical applied linguists have long observed that the global language learning industry operates within a neoliberal framework that treats language as a commodity, learners as consumers, and proficiency as human capital (Block, Gray, & Holborow, 2012; Heller, 2003, 2010; Park & Wee, 2012). AI-powered language learning tools intensify this commodification in several ways.

First, AI tools reduce language learning to a product that can be purchased, consumed, and measured. Duolingo's subscription model, ELSA Speak's premium features, and ChatGPT Plus's advanced voice mode all position language learning as a market transaction: the learner pays for access to a service that delivers measurable "improvements" in proficiency. The metrics that AI tools generate--scores, streaks, accuracy percentages, vocabulary counts--reinforce this transactional framing by representing language learning as a quantifiable return on investment.

Second, AI tools individualize language learning in ways that obscure its social dimensions. The dominant model of AI language learning is the individual learner interacting with an algorithm on a personal device. There is no classroom, no community of co-learners, no shared struggle, no collective meaning-making. This individualization aligns with the neoliberal emphasis on personal responsibility: if you do not learn the language, it is because you did not practice enough, not because you lacked access to communities, faced discrimination, or operated within structures that devalued your existing linguistic repertoire.

Third, AI tools generate enormous quantities of learner data that has market value beyond the language learning context. Usage patterns, proficiency levels, learning trajectories, content preferences, and even speech recordings are collected, stored, and potentially monetized. The learner is not only a consumer but a product: their data fuels the improvement of AI systems and, in many cases, is shared with third parties for advertising, research, or product development. This data extraction adds a new dimension to the commodification of language learning: the learner pays to use the tool, and the tool extracts value from the learner.

### 12.6.2 The Skills Discourse and Its Limits

AI language learning tools are embedded in a "skills discourse" that reduces language proficiency to a set of discrete, measurable skills: vocabulary size, grammar accuracy, pronunciation score, fluency rate. This discourse aligns with the competency-based education movement and with labor market demands for quantifiable credentials. It is also deeply reductive: it strips language of its social, cultural, aesthetic, and identity-forming dimensions, treating it as a technical tool rather than a mode of being in the world.

Kramsch (2006) has argued that language education should aim not merely at communicative competence but at symbolic competence--the ability to understand and manipulate the symbolic dimensions of language, including its power to shape perception, construct reality, and position social subjects. AI language learning tools, oriented as they are toward measurable skills, do not address symbolic competence. They can help a learner produce grammatically correct sentences but cannot help them understand the social meanings those sentences carry, the power dynamics they enact, or the identities they construct. The result is a form of language proficiency that is technically functional but symbolically impoverished--a proficiency that enables transactions but not transformations.

### 12.6.3 Who Benefits?

The neoliberal critique also directs attention to the political economy of AI language learning: who benefits from the massive investment in AI-powered language tools, and who does not? The primary beneficiaries are technology companies (Duolingo's market capitalization exceeds $12 billion as of 2025), venture capital investors, and learners in wealthy countries with reliable internet access and digital literacy. The costs are borne by language teachers (whose labor is devalued or displaced by automation), learners in under-resourced contexts (who lack access to the devices, internet, and digital literacy required to benefit from AI tools), and linguistic communities whose languages are not supported by AI systems (because the market incentive to develop AI tools for low-resource languages is insufficient).

This distributional analysis complicates the celebratory narrative surrounding AI language learning. The narrative of democratized access--"anyone with a smartphone can learn a language for free!"--obscures the significant barriers (digital access, digital literacy, time, motivation, community support) that prevent many potential learners from benefiting. It also obscures the question of what kind of language learning AI tools provide: a scaffolded, community-embedded, identity-affirming experience of becoming bilingual, or a gamified, individualized, data-harvesting simulation of language learning that may or may not produce genuine communicative competence.

## 12.7 Decolonial Perspectives on AI in Language Education

### 12.7.1 AI as Colonial Technology

Decolonial scholars have argued that AI systems, as products of Western technology companies trained primarily on Western data, carry forward the epistemic hierarchies and power relations of colonialism into the digital age (Couldry & Mejias, 2019; Mohamed, Png, & Isaac, 2020; Ricaurte, 2019). In language education specifically, this critique has several dimensions.

First, AI language learning tools reproduce the linguistic hierarchies established by colonialism. English, the language of the former British Empire and current American hegemony, dominates the AI language learning market. Spanish, French, and Portuguese--the other major colonial languages--receive the next tier of investment. Indigenous languages, minority languages, and languages of the Global South receive minimal attention. The market logic is clear: develop AI tools for languages with the largest number of potential paying users. But the result is a technological infrastructure that reinforces the dominance of colonial languages while further marginalizing the languages and linguistic practices of colonized peoples.

Second, the pedagogical approaches embedded in AI language learning tools reflect Western educational assumptions. The emphasis on individual learning, measurable outcomes, standardized norms, and competitive gamification aligns with Western educational values but may be incongruent with educational traditions that emphasize communal learning, oral transmission, relational pedagogy, and holistic development. An AI language tool designed in Silicon Valley and deployed in West Africa or Southeast Asia carries not only the technology but the pedagogical ideology of its origin.

Third, the data practices of AI language learning companies replicate colonial extraction patterns. Data flows from the Global South (where users generate data through their learning activities) to the Global North (where technology companies are headquartered and where the data is stored, processed, and monetized). This "data colonialism" (Couldry & Mejias, 2019) extracts value from communities without returning commensurate benefits, mirroring the resource extraction patterns of historical colonialism.

### 12.7.2 Whose Knowledge? Whose Language?

The decolonial perspective raises fundamental questions about the knowledge systems embedded in AI language tools. When an AI system teaches "Spanish," whose Spanish does it teach? The Spanish of the Real Academia Espanola, which has historically positioned itself as the authority on "correct" Spanish while marginalizing Latin American, Caribbean, and Indigenous-influenced varieties? The Spanish of educated urban speakers, which functions as a prestige norm while rural, working-class, and Indigenous varieties are stigmatized? The answer, overwhelmingly, is the former: AI systems encode prestige varieties as the standard and treat non-prestige varieties as errors, reproducing within the digital realm the same linguistic hierarchies that decolonial scholars have spent decades challenging.

For Indigenous and minoritized languages, the situation is even more stark. When AI tools do support these languages--as Google Translate has expanded to include several hundred languages--the quality is often poor, the training data is scraped from the internet without community consent, and the resulting tools may introduce errors, misrepresentations, or culturally inappropriate translations. Language communities may find their linguistic heritage being processed by AI systems they did not authorize, producing representations of their language they do not recognize, for the benefit of technology companies they have no relationship with.

Canagarajah (2013) and Pennycook (2018) have argued for a "translingual" approach that honors the fluid, hybrid, and locally situated nature of linguistic practice, rather than treating languages as bounded, countable objects that can be taught and tested separately. AI systems, with their reliance on defined language labels, standardized corpora, and monolingual norms, are fundamentally at odds with this translingual vision. They enforce the very linguistic boundaries that decolonial and translingual scholars seek to dissolve.

### 12.7.3 Resistance and Alternative Visions

The decolonial critique does not lead inevitably to the rejection of AI in language education. Rather, it points toward the need for alternative designs--AI tools developed by and for marginalized communities, grounded in local epistemologies, and governed by community priorities rather than market logic.

Examples of such alternatives are emerging. The First Voices platform, developed by Indigenous communities in British Columbia, uses digital technology (though not yet AI) for Indigenous language revitalization under community governance. The Masakhane project develops NLP tools for African languages through a distributed network of African researchers. The Lacuna Fund supports the creation of labeled datasets for languages and contexts underrepresented in existing AI training data. These initiatives point toward a model of AI language technology that is community-led, culturally grounded, and oriented toward linguistic justice rather than market dominance.

For language educators, the decolonial perspective demands a critical consciousness about the AI tools they adopt and recommend. It requires asking: Whose language does this tool teach? Whose norms does it enforce? Whose data does it collect? Who benefits from its use? And are there alternative tools, approaches, or practices that better serve the needs and honor the identities of the learners in their specific context?

## 12.8 Learner Resistance and Appropriation of AI Tools

### 12.8.1 Learners as Agents, Not Recipients

The preceding sections have focused on how AI shapes and constrains learner identity. But learners are not passive recipients of technology; they are agents who actively interpret, resist, appropriate, and repurpose the tools available to them. The history of CALL is replete with examples of learners using technologies in ways their designers never intended: students using spell-checkers not to correct errors but to explore alternative spellings; gamers developing L2 proficiency through gameplay that was not designed for language learning; social media users developing code-switching repertoires through platform-mediated multilingual interaction (Thorne, 2003, 2008).

AI tools are subject to the same processes of learner appropriation. Emerging research and anecdotal evidence suggest several patterns:

**Creative misuse**: Learners use AI tools for purposes beyond their designed function. A learner might use ChatGPT not for grammar practice but to generate fictional dialogues in which they play a character, effectively creating a creative writing and identity exploration exercise from a tool designed for conversation practice. A learner might use an AI translation tool not for translation but for reverse engineering--inputting sentences to see how the AI renders them and using the output to learn about L2 structures.

**Strategic resistance**: Learners who recognize the limitations of AI feedback may develop strategies for working around those limitations. A heritage Spanish speaker who receives unwanted corrections of their home variety might deliberately maintain their variety in AI interactions as an act of linguistic resistance, using the AI as a foil against which to assert their linguistic identity. A learner who finds AI pronunciation feedback unhelpful for their communicative goals might ignore the feedback selectively, practicing the features they consider important and disregarding those they do not.

**Social repurposing**: Learners may integrate AI tools into social learning practices that the tools were not designed to support. Study groups might share AI-generated feedback and discuss it collectively, transforming individual AI feedback into a social learning event. Learners might use AI-generated conversation transcripts as the basis for classroom discussion, analysis, or role-play, embedding AI output in human social interaction.

### 12.8.2 The Appropriation Framework

Thorne's (2003) concept of "cultures of use"--the idea that technologies carry with them expectations about how they should be used, but that these expectations can be resisted or transformed by users from different cultural backgrounds--provides a useful framework for understanding learner appropriation of AI tools. AI language learning tools are designed with specific cultures of use in mind: individual practice, gamified progression, data-driven optimization. Learners who come from educational traditions that emphasize collaborative learning, oral interaction, and narrative meaning-making may find these cultures of use alienating and may appropriate the tools into their own cultural practices in ways that are pedagogically productive.

The challenge for researchers is to move beyond the designer-intended use of AI tools and investigate how learners actually use them in practice. Ethnographic and case study methodologies (Duff, 2008; Canagarajah, 2013) are essential for capturing the situated, creative, and often unexpected ways that learners engage with AI. Such research can inform the design of AI tools that are more amenable to diverse cultures of use--tools that offer flexibility, customization, and social features rather than enforcing a single mode of engagement.

### 12.8.3 The Limits of Appropriation

While learner appropriation is a hopeful narrative, it is important not to romanticize it. Appropriation requires agency, and agency requires resources--cognitive, material, and social. A learner with high digital literacy, strong metacognitive skills, and access to alternative learning resources (human teachers, peer communities, target language media) can appropriate AI tools creatively and critically. A learner who lacks these resources--who has no teacher to provide alternative perspectives, no community of co-learners to share experiences with, and no critical framework for evaluating AI feedback--may be more vulnerable to the constraining effects of AI on identity, agency, and autonomy.

The distribution of appropriation capacity is inequitable. Learners from privileged backgrounds, with access to diverse learning resources and strong educational foundations, are best positioned to use AI tools as supplements to a rich learning ecology. Learners from disadvantaged backgrounds, for whom AI tools may be the primary (or only) learning resource, are most vulnerable to the identity-constraining, autonomy-limiting, and ideologically reproductive effects that this chapter has identified. The digital divide, in this analysis, is not only a divide of access but a divide of appropriation capacity--a divide between learners who can use AI tools on their own terms and learners who are used by them.

## 12.9 Implications for Pedagogy and Design

### 12.9.1 Identity-Aware AI Design

The analysis in this chapter points toward several design principles for AI language learning tools that take identity, agency, and equity seriously:

**Principle 1: Variety recognition.** AI tools should recognize and validate multiple varieties of the target language, allowing learners to choose their target variety and providing feedback that distinguishes between features that affect intelligibility and features that mark variety membership. This requires moving beyond a single standard norm to a pluricentric model of language.

**Principle 2: Learner control over goals.** AI tools should allow learners to define their own learning goals--professional, academic, social, artistic, heritage maintenance--and to configure the system accordingly. The algorithm's optimization function should serve the learner's goals, not the platform's engagement metrics.

**Principle 3: Transparency of norms.** AI tools should make explicit the linguistic norms they encode, the populations their models were trained on, and the limitations of their feedback. This transparency enables the critical awareness that Section 12.5 identified as essential.

**Principle 4: Social integration.** AI tools should be designed for integration into social learning contexts rather than as standalone individual products. Features that support collaborative use--shared workspaces, discussion prompts, peer comparison (optional, not forced), teacher dashboards--can help embed AI practice in the social relationships that drive investment and identity formation.

**Principle 5: Graduated autonomy transfer.** AI tools should be designed to develop learner autonomy over time, gradually transferring decision-making authority from the algorithm to the learner. Early stages may involve high algorithmic guidance; later stages should offer the learner increasing control over content, pace, and practice format, with the explicit goal of making the learner a self-directed language learner who no longer needs the tool.

### 12.9.2 The Teacher's Role in Identity-Affirming AI Integration

Teachers play an irreplaceable role in mediating the relationship between learners and AI tools. They can:

- Help learners develop the critical AI literacy described in Section 12.5, enabling them to use tools reflectively rather than uncritically.
- Provide the human recognition and relational support that AI cannot offer, affirming learners' identities and validating their existing linguistic repertoires.
- Create classroom activities that embed AI practice in social interaction, ensuring that AI use supplements rather than replaces human communication.
- Facilitate discussions about language variation, standard language ideology, and the norms embedded in AI tools, developing the critical language awareness that equips learners to navigate linguistic power dynamics.
- Advocate for learners whose identities are marginalized or pathologized by AI feedback, providing alternative frames of reference and supporting learner resistance to inappropriate norms.

### 12.9.3 Research Directions

The intersection of learner identity, agency, and AI is a nascent research area that demands sustained attention. Priority research questions include:

- How do learners from different L1 and cultural backgrounds experience AI-mediated language learning in terms of identity construction, investment, and agency?
- Does AI-mediated practice lead to different identity outcomes (self-efficacy, language anxiety, sense of belonging) than human-mediated practice?
- How do learners appropriate AI tools in practice, and what conditions (educational, social, material) enable productive appropriation?
- How do AI language tools affect heritage language learners' relationships with their heritage language and culture?
- What design features of AI tools promote or hinder learner autonomy development?
- How do continuous AI assessment and feedback affect learner identity over time--do they promote confidence or dependency?

These questions require methodological approaches that go beyond the quantitative, experiment-based paradigm that dominates AI-SLA research. Longitudinal case studies, ethnographic investigations, narrative inquiry, and participatory action research are essential for capturing the lived experience of learners negotiating identity in AI-mediated environments.

## 12.10 Conclusion

The introduction of AI into language learning is not merely a technological development; it is an intervention in the social, cultural, and political processes through which language learners construct identities, exercise agency, and negotiate belonging. Norton's investment theory reminds us that language learning is driven not by abstract "motivation" but by the learner's understanding of what language proficiency will make possible--what communities it will open, what identities it will enable, what forms of capital it will yield. Darvin and Norton's extension to digital spaces highlights the ideological structures that determine whose language counts, whose identity is valued, and whose investment is rewarded.

AI language learning tools interact with these dynamics in complex and ambivalent ways. They can lower affective barriers, provide safe spaces for identity experimentation, and expand access to L2 practice. But they can also standardize language, enforce monolingual norms, individualize learning in ways that sever it from its social roots, and commodify language education within a neoliberal framework that treats proficiency as human capital rather than human experience. The decolonial critique reveals that these are not incidental features of AI language tools but structural consequences of their development within Western, market-driven technology ecosystems.

The critical perspective is necessary but not sufficient. Learners are not victims of technology; they are agents who resist, appropriate, and transform the tools available to them. The challenge for language educators, researchers, and AI developers is to create the conditions under which productive appropriation is possible for all learners--not only those with the privilege and resources to use AI tools on their own terms but also those who are most vulnerable to the constraining effects of algorithmic language instruction.

This requires a commitment to identity-affirming design, critical digital literacy, variety recognition, learner autonomy, and social integration that goes beyond the current market incentives of the AI language learning industry. It requires, in short, the same commitment to equity, justice, and human flourishing that has animated the best work in critical applied linguistics for decades--now extended to meet the challenges and possibilities of the algorithmic age. The AI tool is not the teacher, not the community, not the identity. It is a mediating artifact (Thorne, 2003) that sits between the learner and their becoming, and its design must honor the complexity of what it mediates.
