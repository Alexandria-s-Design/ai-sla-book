## 4.1 Introduction: The Promise and Peril of Algorithmically Mediated Input

No construct occupies a more central position in second language acquisition theory than *input*. From Krashen's (1985) foundational claim that comprehensible input is the single causal variable in language acquisition to VanPatten's (2004) detailed account of how learners process and parse incoming linguistic data, the field has consistently returned to a core question: What kind of language exposure drives acquisition, and how much of it is needed? The emergence of artificial intelligence systems capable of generating, adapting, and delivering linguistic input at unprecedented scale and personalization would appear, at first glance, to represent the fulfillment of a pedagogical dream. If acquisition requires comprehensible input at the learner's current level plus one step beyond (Krashen's i+1), and if AI can calibrate that input in real time across infinite domains and modalities, then the input problem -- historically one of scarcity, mismatch, and inaccessibility -- might finally be solved.

This chapter argues that the reality is considerably more complicated. While AI-mediated input systems offer genuine affordances that deserve serious scholarly attention, they also introduce theoretical complications that the field has barely begun to address. What counts as "comprehensible" when an algorithm, rather than a human interlocutor attuned to communicative context, determines the difficulty level? What happens to the authenticity of input when it is generated by a statistical model trained on internet-scale corpora rather than produced by a speaker with communicative intent? Does the infinite availability of AI-generated text change the cognitive and motivational dynamics of input processing, or does it merely create the illusion of engagement without the depth of processing that acquisition requires?

To answer these questions, the chapter proceeds through six major sections. First, it revisits the theoretical foundations of input in SLA, establishing the constructs against which AI systems must be evaluated. Second, it examines AI-powered adaptive reading and listening systems that claim to deliver optimally calibrated input. Third, it analyzes AI-generated comprehensible input -- text and speech produced by large language models for pedagogical purposes. Fourth, it explores the multimodal dimensions of AI-mediated input, including video, virtual reality, and integrated text-audio-visual systems. Fifth, it surveys the emerging research evidence on whether AI-mediated input actually facilitates acquisition. Finally, it articulates the limitations and pedagogical concerns that practitioners and researchers must confront.

Throughout, the chapter maintains a critical stance: technology that solves the wrong problem, or that solves the right problem in ways that introduce new ones, is not progress. The goal is not to celebrate or condemn AI-mediated input but to subject it to the same rigorous theoretical scrutiny that the field applies to any claim about the mechanisms of second language acquisition.

## 4.2 Theoretical Foundations: What SLA Theory Demands of Input

### 4.2.1 Krashen's Input Hypothesis and the i+1 Construct

Stephen Krashen's Input Hypothesis, first articulated in the late 1970s and formalized in *The Input Hypothesis: Issues and Implications* (Krashen, 1985), remains the most widely cited -- and most frequently critiqued -- claim about the role of input in SLA. The hypothesis asserts that language acquisition occurs when learners are exposed to input that is slightly beyond their current level of competence, formalized as *i+1*, where *i* represents the learner's current interlanguage stage and *+1* represents the next developmental increment. According to Krashen, this comprehensible input is both necessary and sufficient for acquisition; no amount of explicit instruction, output practice, or grammatical explanation can substitute for exposure to appropriately leveled input in a low-anxiety communicative context.

The elegance of the hypothesis has always been matched by the difficulty of operationalizing it. Krashen himself never provided a precise mechanism for determining what constitutes *+1* for a given learner at a given moment. In classroom contexts, teachers approximate i+1 through professional judgment, familiarity with their students, and the use of graded materials, simplified speech (foreigner talk), and contextual support. The approximation is inherently imprecise, and decades of research have demonstrated that the relationship between input difficulty, learner proficiency, and acquisition is far more complex than the i+1 formulation suggests (Gass & Mackey, 2015; Ortega, 2009).

It is precisely this imprecision that makes the Input Hypothesis attractive to AI developers. If the problem with i+1 is that human teachers cannot reliably calibrate it for every learner at every moment, then an adaptive algorithm that continuously assesses learner proficiency and adjusts input difficulty in real time would seem to offer a solution. This framing, however, misunderstands the nature of the critique. The problem with i+1 is not primarily a calibration problem -- it is a theoretical one. As Long (1996) argued, input alone does not drive acquisition; it is the interaction around input, including negotiation of meaning, that creates the conditions for development. As Swain (1985) argued, comprehending input is not the same as acquiring the forms within it, because comprehension can rely on semantic and contextual cues that bypass the syntactic processing necessary for acquisition. Solving the calibration problem while ignoring the theoretical one produces a more efficient delivery mechanism for an incomplete theory.

### 4.2.2 VanPatten's Input Processing Theory

Bill VanPatten's Input Processing (IP) theory (VanPatten, 1996, 2004, 2015) addresses a question that Krashen's hypothesis does not: How do learners actually process the input they receive? IP theory posits that learners are limited-capacity processors who employ a set of default strategies when parsing input for meaning. These strategies, articulated as a series of principles, explain why certain forms in the input are more likely to be noticed and processed than others. The First Noun Principle, for example, predicts that learners will assign the role of agent to the first noun or pronoun they encounter, regardless of the actual grammatical structure. The Lexical Preference Principle predicts that learners will rely on lexical items (content words) rather than grammatical morphemes when both encode the same meaning.

For AI-mediated input, IP theory raises important questions. If learners employ default processing strategies that can lead them to incorrect interpretations, then simply providing more input -- even perfectly calibrated input -- may not result in the correct form-meaning connections. VanPatten's pedagogical application, Processing Instruction (PI), specifically designs input activities that force learners to abandon their default strategies and attend to target forms. The question for AI systems is whether they can deliver input that achieves this structured processing, or whether they default to providing large quantities of unstructured, meaning-rich text that allows learners to continue relying on their existing (and potentially misleading) processing strategies.

### 4.2.3 Input Enhancement and the Salience Problem

Sharwood Smith's (1991, 1993) concept of input enhancement provides another theoretical lens for evaluating AI-mediated input. Input enhancement refers to any deliberate attempt to make specific features of the input more perceptible to learners -- through textual enhancement (bolding, underlining, color coding), intonational stress, frequency manipulation, or explicit flagging. The underlying assumption, closely related to Schmidt's (1990, 2001) Noticing Hypothesis, is that learners must consciously notice a form in the input before it can become intake -- the subset of input that is actually processed and stored.

AI systems have significant potential for input enhancement. A reading application could highlight target grammatical structures in color, provide hover-over glosses, or increase the frequency of specific forms beyond their natural occurrence rate. A listening application could use prosodic manipulation to emphasize target phonological features. However, the research on input enhancement has produced mixed results even in human-delivered contexts (Lee & Huang, 2008; Jahan & Jahan, 2011), and the addition of AI-driven enhancement introduces new variables: Does the learner attend to the AI's enhancement cues, or do they become visual noise in an already cluttered digital interface? Does algorithmic highlighting of forms disrupt the natural reading or listening process in ways that reduce comprehension rather than supporting it?

### 4.2.4 Nation's Four Strands and the Role of Extensive Input

Paul Nation's (2007) Four Strands framework argues that a well-balanced language program should include roughly equal proportions of four types of activity: meaning-focused input, meaning-focused output, language-focused learning (deliberate study), and fluency development. The meaning-focused input strand, which encompasses extensive reading and extensive listening, requires that learners encounter large quantities of input at a comfortable level of difficulty -- typically with 95-98% vocabulary coverage (Nation, 2001; Hu & Nation, 2000).

This framework is particularly relevant to AI-mediated input because AI systems excel at generating large volumes of text and can, in principle, control vocabulary load with precision. The extensive reading research tradition, led by Day and Bamford (2002) and further developed by Waring and McLean (2015), has accumulated substantial evidence that sustained engagement with large quantities of level-appropriate text promotes vocabulary acquisition, reading fluency, and general language proficiency. If AI can generate an infinite library of graded readers tailored to individual learner interests and proficiency levels, the implications for extensive reading programs are significant.

Yet the extensive reading literature also emphasizes factors that AI cannot easily replicate: the motivational power of choosing one's own reading material, the pleasure of narrative engagement, the social dimensions of discussing books with peers, and the role of the teacher as a reading model and advisor (Day & Bamford, 2002). These affective and social dimensions are not incidental to the extensive reading approach; they are constitutive of it. An AI system that generates perfectly leveled text but fails to engage the learner as a reader -- as someone who reads because reading is intrinsically rewarding -- may produce input without producing the conditions for intake.

## 4.3 Adaptive Reading and Listening Systems: The Calibration Promise

### 4.3.1 How Adaptive Systems Work

Adaptive reading and listening systems represent one of the most developed applications of AI in language input delivery. These systems use a combination of natural language processing, learner modeling, and content generation or selection algorithms to adjust the difficulty of reading or listening materials in real time based on inferred learner proficiency.

The typical architecture involves three components. First, a *learner model* tracks the individual's vocabulary knowledge, reading speed, comprehension accuracy, and sometimes eye-tracking or interaction patterns to estimate current proficiency. Second, a *content analysis engine* applies readability metrics, vocabulary frequency analysis, syntactic complexity measures, and discourse structure analysis to characterize the difficulty of available texts. Third, a *matching algorithm* selects or generates texts that fall within the learner's estimated zone of proximal development -- the AI analogue of i+1.

Early systems relied on relatively crude readability formulas (Flesch-Kincaid, Coleman-Liau) that correlated poorly with actual comprehension difficulty for L2 readers (Crossley, Greenfield, & McNamara, 2008). More recent systems use NLP-based text complexity measures such as Coh-Metrix (McNamara, Graesser, McCarthy, & Zareva, 2011), which analyze cohesion, referential density, syntactic structure, and word frequency simultaneously. Machine learning classifiers trained on learner interaction data can predict text difficulty for specific populations with considerably greater accuracy than formula-based approaches (Vajjala & Meurers, 2012; Xia, Kochmar, & Briscoe, 2016).

### 4.3.2 Commercial Implementations

Several commercial platforms have implemented adaptive input systems with varying degrees of sophistication. Duolingo, the largest language learning application globally with over 500 million registered users, uses a machine learning model called Birdbrain to estimate learner word knowledge and adjust lesson content accordingly (Settles & Meeder, 2016). The system tracks each learner's interaction history to build a probabilistic model of which words and structures have been acquired and which are ready for introduction. Newsela, while designed primarily for L1 literacy, offers leveled versions of authentic news articles at multiple Lexile levels and has been adapted for ESL contexts. LingQ provides extensive reading materials with integrated vocabulary tracking and uses learner-marked word knowledge to filter and recommend texts.

More recently, large language model-based systems have emerged that generate reading and listening materials dynamically. Applications such as Languable and various GPT-powered tutoring platforms can produce texts on any topic at a specified difficulty level, theoretically providing unlimited input tailored to individual interests and proficiency. Google's Reading Tutor, released in 2024, uses speech recognition and language models to provide real-time oral reading support for developing readers, adjusting text difficulty based on accuracy and fluency metrics.

### 4.3.3 Critical Evaluation of Adaptive Systems

The promise of adaptive systems is real but must be evaluated against several critical concerns. First, the *learner model* in these systems is necessarily reductive. Proficiency is multidimensional -- a learner may have strong vocabulary knowledge but weak syntactic processing, or high reading comprehension but low listening comprehension. Most adaptive systems reduce this multidimensionality to a single difficulty parameter, which may result in text that is too easy on some dimensions and too difficult on others.

Second, the *difficulty metric* itself is contested. Readability research has demonstrated that text difficulty for L2 readers depends not only on linguistic features but also on background knowledge, genre familiarity, cultural schemata, and the reader's L1 typological distance from the L2 (Bernhardt, 2011). An AI system that calibrates difficulty based purely on linguistic features may systematically miscalibrate for learners whose background knowledge creates unexpected comprehension patterns. A Spanish-speaking learner reading English texts about Latin American history, for instance, may find a linguistically complex text quite accessible due to background knowledge, while a linguistically simple text about cricket may be incomprehensible due to cultural unfamiliarity.

Third, the pedagogical assumption underlying adaptive systems -- that matching difficulty to proficiency maximizes learning -- is not uncontested. Vygotsky's (1978) zone of proximal development suggests that learning occurs when the learner is stretched beyond current independent capability but supported by a more capable other. In the adaptive reading context, the "more capable other" is the algorithm, and the "support" is the difficulty calibration itself. But Vygotskian theory also emphasizes that the mediation occurs through social interaction -- through the co-construction of meaning between persons. An algorithm that adjusts text difficulty is providing a form of scaffolding, but it is not engaging in the dialogic, reciprocal mediation that sociocultural theory considers essential to development.

### 4.3.4 The Goldilocks Illusion

There is a deeper epistemological concern with adaptive input systems that merits explicit articulation. These systems assume that there exists, for any given learner at any given moment, an optimal difficulty level -- a "Goldilocks zone" -- and that the system's task is to find it. This assumption maps neatly onto Krashen's i+1 but sits uncomfortably with more dynamic models of acquisition. Complex Dynamic Systems Theory (CDST), as articulated by Larsen-Freeman and Cameron (2008) and de Bot, Lowie, and Verspoor (2007), views language development not as a linear progression through ordered stages but as an emergent, nonlinear process characterized by variability, self-organization, and sensitive dependence on initial conditions. In a CDST framework, there is no single optimal input level; development is driven by the interaction of multiple subsystems (lexical, syntactic, phonological, pragmatic, motivational) that may be at different stages at any given moment and that influence each other in unpredictable ways.

If CDST is correct -- and the evidence for variability and nonlinearity in L2 development is substantial (Verspoor, de Bot, & Lowie, 2011; Lowie & Verspoor, 2019) -- then adaptive systems that seek a single optimal difficulty level are solving for a variable that may not exist as theorized. The learner who encounters a text that is "too difficult" on one dimension may be catalyzed by that very difficulty to reorganize their interlanguage in ways that an optimally calibrated text would never provoke. The productive role of confusion, surprise, and destabilization in cognitive development -- documented in both constructivist (Piaget) and dynamic systems frameworks -- may be precisely what adaptive systems are designed to eliminate.

## 4.4 AI-Generated Comprehensible Input: Authenticity, Quality, and the Question of Intent

### 4.4.1 Large Language Models as Input Generators

The advent of large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), Claude (Anthropic, 2024), Gemini (Google DeepMind, 2024), and open-source models like LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023) has introduced a fundamentally new category of language input: text generated by statistical models trained on hundreds of billions of words of human-produced text. These models can produce reading passages on virtually any topic, at virtually any difficulty level, in virtually any genre, in a matter of seconds. A language teacher can prompt an LLM to "Write a 500-word news article about climate change at a B1 level" and receive a grammatically correct, topically relevant, and apparently coherent text almost instantaneously.

This capability has obvious pedagogical applications. Teachers who previously spent hours searching for appropriately leveled authentic texts can now generate custom materials on demand. Learners can request texts about topics that interest them at their self-assessed proficiency level. Language programs can produce large banks of reading and listening materials without the copyright concerns associated with authentic texts. The efficiency gains are undeniable.

### 4.4.2 The Authenticity Problem

Yet the question of authenticity, which has occupied CALL researchers since the earliest days of the field, takes on new dimensions when the input is generated by an AI rather than selected from existing human-produced texts. Authenticity in language teaching has been defined in multiple ways. Widdowson (1979) distinguished between *genuineness* (the text was produced by a real speaker for a real audience) and *authenticity* (the text engages the learner in genuine communicative activity). Gilmore (2007) identified multiple dimensions of authenticity, including the source, the interaction, the task, and the cultural context. By most of these definitions, AI-generated text presents a problematic case.

An LLM-generated reading passage is not *genuine* in Widdowson's sense: it was not produced by a speaker with communicative intent for a real audience. It is a statistical approximation of what such a text might look like, assembled by predicting the most probable next token given the preceding context. The text may be grammatically flawless and topically relevant, but it lacks the pragmatic grounding of genuine communication. A news article generated by GPT-4 has no journalist behind it who investigated, verified, and deliberately structured information for a readership. A story generated by Claude has no author who chose words for their aesthetic or emotional resonance.

Does this matter for acquisition? The answer depends on one's theoretical commitments. If acquisition is driven primarily by exposure to comprehensible linguistic data -- if what matters is the form of the input rather than its communicative context -- then AI-generated text may serve just as well as human-produced text. This is essentially the position implied by Krashen's Input Hypothesis. But if acquisition is mediated by the learner's engagement with meaning in communicative contexts -- if understanding the speaker's intent, interpreting pragmatic cues, and responding to genuine communicative pressure are part of what drives intake -- then the absence of communicative intent in AI-generated text may represent a fundamental limitation.

### 4.4.3 Linguistic Quality of AI-Generated Input

Beyond the philosophical question of authenticity, there is the empirical question of linguistic quality. Research on LLM output has documented several recurrent features that have implications for L2 input quality:

**Lexical homogeneity**. LLMs tend to favor high-frequency vocabulary and common collocations, producing text that is lexically less diverse than equivalent human-produced text (Shaib et al., 2024). For extensive reading purposes, where vocabulary acquisition depends on encountering infrequent but informative words in context, this homogeneity may limit learning opportunities.

**Register flattening**. LLMs that have been fine-tuned with reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) tend to converge on a neutral, helpful register that lacks the stylistic variation found in authentic texts (Bender, Gebru, McMillan-Major, & Shmitchell, 2021; Perez et al., 2022). A learner reading AI-generated texts across multiple genres may receive inadequate exposure to the register variation that characterizes natural language use and that is itself a target of advanced L2 competence.

**Pragmatic infelicity**. While LLMs produce grammatically well-formed text, they frequently produce text that is pragmatically odd -- that says things a speaker would not actually say in the purported communicative context (Ruis et al., 2023). For L2 learners who are simultaneously developing grammatical and pragmatic competence, exposure to pragmatically infelicitous input could be actively misleading.

**Cultural superficiality**. When asked to produce culturally situated texts, LLMs often produce stereotypical or surface-level cultural representations that lack the depth, specificity, and nuance of texts produced by cultural insiders (Bender et al., 2021). For language learners, who are acquiring not just linguistic forms but cultural knowledge and intercultural competence, this superficiality is a significant limitation.

### 4.4.4 Graded Readers and AI: A Case Study

The traditional graded reader -- a narrative text written within controlled vocabulary and grammatical parameters for language learners at specific proficiency levels -- represents a well-established genre with a substantial evidence base. Research by Waring and McLean (2015), Nakanishi (2015), and Jeon and Day (2016) has demonstrated that sustained reading of graded readers promotes vocabulary acquisition, reading fluency, and general proficiency, particularly when reading is extensive (high volume), self-selected, and pleasurable.

AI systems can generate graded readers with precise vocabulary control. An LLM prompted with a specific word list and a maximum sentence length can produce narrative texts that conform to the parameters of any grading scheme. Several startups have begun offering AI-generated graded reader libraries, and major publishers have explored AI-assisted grading of authentic texts.

However, the graded reader research also reveals factors that AI generation cannot easily replicate. The narrative quality of graded readers -- the ability to tell a compelling story within severe linguistic constraints -- is a craft that the best graded reader authors (e.g., the Oxford Bookworms writers) have spent decades developing. AI-generated narratives at lower proficiency levels, where the vocabulary and structural constraints are most severe, tend to be flat, predictable, and lacking in the narrative tension that motivates continued reading (Crossley, Kyle, & McNamara, 2016). If the motivational component of extensive reading is essential to its efficacy -- and the evidence suggests it is (Day & Bamford, 2002; Nishino, 2007) -- then AI-generated graded readers that are technically well-calibrated but narratively unengaging may not deliver the same acquisition benefits.

There is also an equity dimension. AI graded reader generators are most effective in English and other high-resource languages where training data is abundant. For less-resourced languages, the quality of AI-generated text degrades significantly, and for many indigenous and minority languages, LLMs cannot generate coherent text at all (Joshi et al., 2020). The promise of "infinite input" is thus geographically and linguistically constrained in ways that may widen rather than narrow existing inequalities in language education resources.

## 4.5 Multimodal Input: Text, Audio, Video, and Immersive Environments

### 4.5.1 Mayer's Cognitive Theory and AI-Mediated Multimodality

Richard Mayer's (2009) Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML) provides a framework for understanding how learners process information presented across multiple modalities. CTML's dual-channel assumption -- that humans process visual and auditory information through separate channels with limited capacity -- has direct implications for the design of AI-mediated language input. Effective multimedia input, according to Mayer, presents information in ways that manage cognitive load across channels, using the coherence principle (eliminate extraneous material), the signaling principle (highlight essential material), the redundancy principle (avoid presenting identical information in multiple modalities simultaneously), and the modality principle (present words as narration rather than on-screen text when accompanied by images).

AI systems are increasingly capable of generating and coordinating multimodal input. Text-to-speech models (e.g., OpenAI's Whisper and TTS systems, ElevenLabs) can convert any written text into natural-sounding audio in multiple languages and voices. Image generation models (DALL-E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion) can create visual illustrations for vocabulary and reading materials. Video generation models (Sora, Runway) can produce animated scenes that contextualize linguistic input. The integration of these modalities into a unified learning experience -- where a learner reads a text, hears it narrated, sees illustrative images, and watches contextualizing video, all calibrated to their proficiency level -- represents a qualitative leap beyond what traditional language teaching materials can offer.

### 4.5.2 AI-Powered Captioning and Subtitling

One of the most empirically supported applications of multimodal input in L2 learning is captioned video. Decades of research have demonstrated that L2 learners benefit from watching video with captions, particularly same-language captions (Vanderplank, 2010; Montero Perez, Van Den Noortgate, & Desmet, 2013). The combination of aural and written input provides redundant linguistic information that supports both comprehension and form-meaning mapping. Learners who watch captioned video show gains in vocabulary acquisition, listening comprehension, and phonological awareness compared to those who watch without captions or with L1 subtitles (Montero Perez et al., 2013).

AI has dramatically improved the availability and accuracy of video captions. Automatic speech recognition (ASR) systems such as OpenAI's Whisper (Radford et al., 2023) have reached near-human accuracy for high-resource languages, making it possible to caption any video in real time. YouTube's auto-captioning system, powered by ASR, provides captions for hundreds of millions of videos across dozens of languages. For language learners, this means that virtually any authentic video content -- news broadcasts, podcasts, lectures, interviews, entertainment -- can be captioned and thus made more accessible as input.

However, ASR accuracy is not uniform across languages, accents, or speech conditions. For less-resourced languages, ASR accuracy remains significantly lower, and for accented speech -- precisely the variety of input that L2 learners need exposure to in preparation for real-world communication -- error rates increase substantially (Koenecke et al., 2020). A learner of English who relies on AI-generated captions for an Indian English podcast or a Jamaican Creole interview may receive captions that are inaccurate in precisely the areas where the input is most valuable for developing perceptual flexibility. Furthermore, the captions may normalize toward the standard variety on which the ASR model was trained, silently erasing the sociolinguistic variation that is itself a learning target.

### 4.5.3 Virtual Reality and Immersive Input

Virtual reality (VR) and augmented reality (AR) platforms represent the most ambitious attempt to create immersive AI-mediated input environments. Applications such as ImmerseMe, Mondly VR, and various research prototypes place learners in simulated environments -- a market, a restaurant, a doctor's office -- where they interact with AI-powered virtual characters in the target language. The theoretical appeal is straightforward: immersive environments provide the rich contextual information, multimodal input, and quasi-authentic communicative situations that SLA theory has long identified as facilitative of acquisition.

Sykes and Reinhardt (2013) proposed that game-mediated and immersive environments can provide the conditions for what they term "experiential learning" in the L2 -- learning through doing, exploring, and making meaningful choices in the target language. Thorne and Hellermann (2015) have argued that embodied interaction in virtual environments can support the development of interactional competence in ways that traditional classroom settings cannot. The addition of AI-powered interlocutors to these environments extends the possibilities further: the virtual market vendor can negotiate price, respond to unexpected questions, and adjust their speech complexity based on the learner's proficiency.

The research evidence, however, is more cautious than the marketing claims. A meta-analysis by Lin and Lan (2015) found that VR-based language learning produced moderate effect sizes for vocabulary acquisition and speaking but that the novelty effect was significant and potentially inflated short-term results. Parmaxi (2023) reviewed VR language learning studies published between 2017 and 2022 and concluded that while learners reported high motivation and engagement, long-term acquisition outcomes were inconsistent, and methodological rigor was often lacking. The cognitive load of navigating a virtual environment while simultaneously processing L2 input may, for some learners, exceed the available processing capacity, violating the limited-capacity assumption of both CTML and VanPatten's Input Processing theory.

There is also the access question. VR headsets remain expensive and may produce motion sickness in a significant minority of users (Chang, Kim, & Yoo, 2020). Mobile-based AR applications are more accessible but offer less immersive experiences. The digital divide that Warschauer (2003) identified two decades ago persists in the immersive technology space, and language programs serving low-resource communities are least likely to have access to the hardware required for VR-based input delivery.

### 4.5.4 The Integration Challenge

The multimodal affordances of AI are genuinely impressive, but integration -- the coordination of multiple modalities into a coherent, pedagogically sound input experience -- remains a significant challenge. Current AI systems can generate text, speech, images, and video independently, but the coordination of these modalities into an experience that manages cognitive load, supports comprehension, and promotes acquisition requires instructional design expertise that AI systems do not possess autonomously. The risk is that multimodal AI input becomes a "kitchen sink" experience -- a barrage of text, audio, images, and video that overwhelms the learner's processing capacity rather than supporting it.

Mayer's (2009) research has repeatedly demonstrated that more modalities do not automatically mean better learning. The redundancy principle, for instance, warns against presenting identical information in both written and spoken form simultaneously, as this can split attention and increase extraneous cognitive load. AI systems that generate a reading passage, narrate it aloud, illustrate each paragraph with a generated image, and play background music may be violating multiple multimedia learning principles even as they showcase impressive technical capabilities.

## 4.6 Research Evidence: Does AI-Mediated Input Facilitate Acquisition?

### 4.6.1 Adaptive Systems and Learning Outcomes

The empirical evidence on AI-adaptive input systems and L2 acquisition outcomes is growing but remains methodologically uneven. Several large-scale studies of Duolingo users have examined learning outcomes, with results that are cautiously positive. Vesselinov and Grego (2012) found that 34 hours of Duolingo use was equivalent to one university semester of Spanish, though the study has been criticized for methodological limitations including a self-selected sample and lack of a control group. Jiang et al. (2021) used a more rigorous design to demonstrate that Duolingo's adaptive algorithm produced greater vocabulary retention than a non-adaptive control condition, though the effect sizes were small to moderate.

Studies of AI-powered reading platforms have shown mixed results. Hsu (2013) found that Taiwanese EFL students using an adaptive reading system showed greater gains in reading comprehension than those using non-adaptive materials, but the gains were limited to lower-proficiency learners. Hsieh, Wu, and Marek (2017) found that adaptive reading systems improved motivation and self-efficacy but did not produce statistically significant gains in reading proficiency over a 12-week period. A recurring finding across studies is that adaptive systems appear to be most beneficial for learners at lower proficiency levels, where the gap between available input and learner ability is greatest, and least beneficial for advanced learners, who may benefit more from the challenge and unpredictability of non-adapted authentic input.

### 4.6.2 AI-Generated Text and Comprehension

Research specifically examining the effects of AI-generated text on L2 comprehension and acquisition is still in its infancy, reflecting the recency of LLM availability. Early studies have focused primarily on comparing the readability and quality of AI-generated texts with human-produced texts at equivalent levels.

Crossley, Kim, Allen, and McNamara (2023) analyzed texts generated by GPT-4 at specified CEFR levels and found that while the texts generally conformed to the targeted level in terms of vocabulary frequency and sentence length, they exhibited less syntactic diversity and less cohesive complexity than human-produced texts at equivalent levels. The AI-generated texts were, in effect, "too clean" -- they lacked the natural variation, repair structures, and complexity patterns that characterize authentic discourse. Whether this matters for acquisition is an open empirical question, but the finding suggests that learners receiving only AI-generated input may be exposed to a narrower range of linguistic structures than those reading authentic texts.

Tai and Chen (2023) conducted an experimental study comparing Chinese EFL students' vocabulary acquisition from AI-generated versus human-authored graded readers. They found no significant difference in immediate post-test vocabulary scores but a significant advantage for human-authored texts on a delayed post-test, suggesting that the AI-generated texts supported short-term recognition but not long-term retention. The authors attributed this finding to the greater contextual richness and narrative engagement of the human-authored texts, which may have promoted deeper processing.

### 4.6.3 Captioned Video and ASR-Supported Listening

The research on AI-captioned video builds on the strong evidence base for captioned video in general. Studies specifically examining auto-generated captions (as opposed to manually produced captions) have found that caption accuracy is a critical moderating variable. Gass, Winke, Isbell, and Ahn (2019) demonstrated that inaccurate captions not only failed to support comprehension but actively hindered it, as learners attempted to reconcile the spoken and written input. Given that ASR accuracy varies substantially across languages, accents, and speech conditions, the benefits of AI captioning are not uniformly distributed.

More positively, Sydorenko (2010) and others have shown that the combination of video, audio, and captions produces a multimodal input experience that supports word-form-meaning mapping more effectively than any single modality alone. As ASR accuracy improves and AI captioning becomes more reliable, the potential for expanding access to captioned authentic video is substantial.

### 4.6.4 The Measurement Problem

A persistent methodological challenge in evaluating AI-mediated input systems is the difficulty of isolating the input variable from other confounding factors. In adaptive systems, the input difficulty is confounded with learner engagement, time on task, and the adaptive algorithm's feedback mechanisms. In studies of AI-generated text, the comparison with human-authored text is confounded by differences in genre, style, and topic interest. In captioned video studies, the captioning modality is confounded with the visual and auditory properties of the video itself.

Chapelle's (2001) criteria for evaluating CALL -- language learning potential, learner fit, meaning focus, authenticity, positive impact, and practicality -- provide a useful framework for evaluating AI-mediated input, but operationalizing these criteria for AI systems requires attention to the dynamic, adaptive, and generative properties that distinguish AI from static CALL materials. New methodological approaches, including learning analytics, process tracing, and dynamic assessment (discussed in Chapter 14), will be necessary to capture the full picture of how learners engage with and learn from AI-mediated input.

## 4.7 Limitations and Pedagogical Concerns

### 4.7.1 The Overabundance Problem

The history of language teaching technology is often narrated as a story of overcoming scarcity: not enough native speakers, not enough authentic texts, not enough exposure time. AI appears to solve the scarcity problem by providing unlimited input. But the transition from scarcity to abundance introduces new problems. Cognitive science has long recognized that more information is not always better. The "paradox of choice" (Schwartz, 2004), the "information overload" phenomenon (Eppler & Mengis, 2004), and the research on attention and filtering in digital environments (Rosen, 2012) all suggest that unlimited input may overwhelm rather than support the learner.

For L2 acquisition specifically, the concern is that the abundance of available input may reduce the depth of processing that leads to intake. A learner who can generate a new reading passage at any moment may not engage in the repeated, effortful reading of a single text that Schmidt's (1990) Noticing Hypothesis and Craik and Lockhart's (1972) Depth of Processing framework suggest is necessary for durable learning. The "extensive" in extensive reading is meant to describe sustained engagement with large quantities of comprehensible text, not rapid consumption of disposable content.

### 4.7.2 The Feedback Vacuum

Input, in natural language acquisition, does not exist in isolation from interaction and feedback. When a child hears a sentence they do not understand, they express confusion, and the caregiver rephrases. When a language student reads a text and misunderstands a passage, the teacher notices and intervenes. This interplay between input and feedback is central to both the Interaction Hypothesis (Long, 1996) and sociocultural accounts of language development (Lantolf & Thorne, 2006).

Many AI-mediated input systems, however, provide input without the accompanying interactional feedback loop. A reading application presents a text and checks comprehension through multiple-choice questions, but it does not respond to the learner's confusion in the nuanced, contingent way that a human interlocutor would. A listening application plays audio and verifies comprehension, but it does not rephrase, elaborate, or negotiate meaning in response to comprehension failure. The input is delivered, but the conditions for converting input to intake -- the interactional push-and-pull that makes specific forms salient and necessary -- may be absent.

### 4.7.3 The Agency Question

A critical pedagogical concern with AI-mediated input is the potential reduction of learner agency. When an algorithm selects reading material, determines difficulty level, decides which vocabulary to highlight, and controls the pace of content delivery, the learner is positioned as a consumer of algorithmically curated input rather than as an agent who actively seeks, selects, and engages with the linguistic environment. This positioning may be particularly problematic in light of the research on autonomy and motivation in language learning (Ushioda, 2011; Benson, 2011), which has consistently found that learners who exercise agency in their learning process are more motivated and more successful than those who follow prescribed curricula passively.

The self-determination theory framework (Deci & Ryan, 1985, 2000), which has been widely applied in language learning motivation research (Noels, Pelletier, Clement, & Vallerand, 2000), identifies autonomy, competence, and relatedness as basic psychological needs that must be satisfied for intrinsic motivation to flourish. AI-adaptive systems may support the competence need by calibrating difficulty to the learner's level, but they may undermine the autonomy need by making choices on the learner's behalf. The relatedness need -- the need for connection with others -- is by definition unmet when input is delivered by an algorithm rather than shared in a social context.

### 4.7.4 The Equity Dimension

Finally, the equity implications of AI-mediated input deserve sustained attention. As noted earlier, AI systems perform best for high-resource languages (English, Spanish, Mandarin, French, German) where training data is abundant, and their quality degrades significantly for the thousands of languages spoken by the majority of the world's population (Joshi et al., 2020). The promise of "personalized input for every learner" is in practice a promise that can be fulfilled only for learners of specific languages. For learners of indigenous languages, minority languages, and less-resourced varieties, AI-mediated input ranges from unreliable to nonexistent.

Moreover, access to AI language learning tools is itself unevenly distributed. Subscription-based platforms require financial resources. Advanced AI systems require computational infrastructure (reliable internet, modern devices) that is unavailable in many educational contexts worldwide. The digital divide in language education, documented by Warschauer (2003) and Thorne (2003), is not merely reproduced but potentially amplified by AI-mediated input systems that offer transformative experiences to the well-resourced while leaving others further behind.

## 4.8 Toward a Critical Pedagogy of AI-Mediated Input

### 4.8.1 Principles for Practice

The preceding analysis suggests a set of principles for the pedagogically sound use of AI-mediated input in L2 contexts:

1. **AI input should supplement, not replace, authentic communicative input.** The efficiency of AI-generated text does not compensate for the absence of genuine communicative context. Learners need exposure to language produced by speakers with communicative intent in real social situations.

2. **Adaptive calibration should be transparent and learner-controlled.** Rather than making difficulty decisions opaquely, AI systems should show learners how difficulty is being adjusted and allow them to override algorithmic choices. Autonomy support is not a luxury feature; it is a motivational necessity.

3. **Multimodal input should follow multimedia learning principles.** More modalities are not automatically better. AI-mediated input experiences should be designed in accordance with Mayer's (2009) principles, managing cognitive load rather than maximizing sensory stimulation.

4. **AI-generated text should be used with awareness of its limitations.** Teachers and learners should understand that AI-generated text is lexically less diverse, pragmatically less rich, and culturally less nuanced than equivalent human-produced text. It is a starting point, not a replacement.

5. **Equity must be a design consideration, not an afterthought.** AI input systems should be evaluated not only by how well they serve their best-case users but by whom they exclude and how.

### 4.8.2 The Role of the Teacher

The most important pedagogical implication of this chapter may be the simplest: AI-mediated input does not eliminate the need for a human teacher. The teacher provides what the algorithm cannot -- the responsive, contextual, socially situated mediation of input that transforms exposure into acquisition. The teacher notices when a learner is disengaged, selects a text because it connects to the learner's life experience, facilitates a discussion that makes a linguistic form salient, and models the engaged reading and listening behavior that motivates continued input processing.

In a learning ecology that includes AI-mediated input, the teacher's role shifts but does not diminish. The teacher becomes a curator, evaluating AI-generated and AI-selected materials for quality, appropriateness, and alignment with learning goals. The teacher becomes a mediator, helping learners make sense of AI-provided input in the interactional context that SLA theory identifies as essential. The teacher becomes an advocate, ensuring that the algorithmic optimization of input serves the learner's development rather than the platform's engagement metrics.

### 4.8.3 Directions for Research

Several research questions emerge from this chapter's analysis that deserve sustained empirical investigation:

1. Does the quality of AI-generated text (as measured by lexical diversity, syntactic complexity, pragmatic appropriateness, and cultural authenticity) affect L2 acquisition outcomes differentially across proficiency levels?

2. How do learners process AI-generated input compared to human-produced input of equivalent linguistic difficulty? Are there differences in depth of processing, noticing of target forms, or engagement as measured by behavioral and physiological indices?

3. What is the relationship between algorithmic input calibration and learner agency? Do learners who exercise choice over their input materials show different acquisition trajectories than those whose input is algorithmically selected?

4. How can adaptive input systems be designed to account for the multidimensionality of proficiency, including the interaction of linguistic knowledge, background knowledge, and sociocultural factors?

5. What are the long-term effects of sustained exposure to AI-mediated input on learners' attitudes toward reading, listening, and language use more broadly?

These questions require methodological approaches that go beyond the short-term, product-oriented experimental designs that have dominated CALL research. Longitudinal studies, mixed-methods designs, learning analytics, and design-based research (discussed in Chapter 14) will be essential for understanding the complex dynamics of AI-mediated input processing.

## 4.9 Conclusion

AI has the potential to transform the provision of linguistic input in ways that align with longstanding goals of SLA theory: more input, better calibrated, more accessible, more diverse. The adaptive systems, generative models, and multimodal platforms examined in this chapter represent genuine advances that deserve serious attention from researchers and practitioners. At the same time, the theoretical analysis reveals that the relationship between AI-mediated input and acquisition is far more complex than the "personalized, unlimited, comprehensible input" marketing narrative suggests.

The core insight of this chapter is that input is not merely a delivery problem. Krashen was right that comprehensible input is necessary for acquisition, but the decades of research since the Input Hypothesis was first proposed have made clear that the conditions under which input becomes intake -- the cognitive, interactional, social, and motivational conditions -- cannot be reduced to a difficulty calibration algorithm. AI can generate text at any level; it cannot ensure that the learner processes that text in ways that drive restructuring of the interlanguage system. AI can present multimodal input across channels; it cannot manage the cognitive load that such presentation imposes on a limited-capacity processor. AI can provide infinite reading material; it cannot replace the human interlocutor whose responsive, contingent mediation transforms comprehensible input into comprehended -- and acquired -- language.

The chapters that follow will take up these themes in the contexts of interaction (Chapter 5), output (Chapter 6), and feedback (Chapter 7), demonstrating that the SLA processing pipeline is a integrated system in which no single component -- not even the foundational component of input -- can be understood in isolation from the others. AI's promise lies not in perfecting any single component but in supporting the complex, dynamic, deeply human process of second language acquisition as a whole.
