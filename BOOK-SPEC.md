## 2. Target Audience and Market Analysis

### Primary Audiences

1. **Applied linguistics researchers and graduate students** (MA/PhD programs in SLA, TESOL, applied linguistics) -- the core academic readership. Programs at institutions such as Georgetown, Teachers College, University of Hawai'i, Michigan State, and Carnegie Mellon enroll thousands of graduate students annually who require current scholarship on technology-mediated language learning.

2. **TESOL/ESL/EFL practitioners** -- classroom teachers, curriculum designers, and language program coordinators seeking research-grounded guidance on integrating AI tools into instruction. The global English Language Teaching (ELT) market exceeds $60 billion, with AI integration emerging as the dominant professional development topic since 2023.

3. **Educational technology researchers** -- scholars investigating human-computer interaction, adaptive learning systems, intelligent tutoring, and learning analytics as applied to language domains.

4. **Language program administrators** -- directors of intensive English programs (IEPs), world language departments, and corporate language training programs who must make adoption decisions about AI-powered platforms.

### Market Analysis

The intersection of AI and SLA represents one of the fastest-growing subfields in applied linguistics. Key market indicators:

- **CALICO Journal**, **ReCALL**, **Language Learning & Technology**, and **Computer Assisted Language Learning** have published a combined 200+ articles on AI and language learning since 2023, yet no single-author monograph provides a comprehensive theoretical and practical synthesis.
- Major professional organizations (TESOL International, ACTFL, CALICO, EuroCALL) have featured AI-focused strands at every annual conference since 2024.
- Graduate programs are adding AI-and-language courses (e.g., Georgetown's "AI in Language Education," MIT's "Computational Approaches to Linguistics") but lack a unified textbook.
- The global AI-in-education market is projected to reach $30 billion by 2028 (HolonIQ), with language learning as the largest consumer-facing segment.

### Market Gap

No existing title bridges SLA theory, AI/NLP technical literacy, and practitioner guidance in a single volume authored by someone with credentials in both educational technology and language acquisition. Current books tend to be either (a) edited collections with uneven quality, (b) practitioner guides lacking theoretical depth, or (c) computational linguistics texts inaccessible to language educators.

## 3. Unique Value Proposition

This book fills a critical gap by providing the first comprehensive, single-author academic monograph that:

1. **Grounds AI applications in established SLA theory** -- Every AI tool and approach is evaluated against canonical frameworks (Krashen's Monitor Model, Long's Interaction Hypothesis, Swain's Output Hypothesis, VanPatten's Input Processing, Sociocultural Theory, Usage-Based approaches, Complex Dynamic Systems Theory) rather than treating AI as a standalone phenomenon.

2. **Provides technical literacy without requiring computer science prerequisites** -- Chapters on NLP, LLMs, speech recognition, and adaptive algorithms are written for language researchers and practitioners, not engineers. The reader will understand *how* these systems work at a conceptual level sufficient for critical evaluation and research design.

3. **Integrates the author's dual expertise** -- Dr. Martin holds an Ed.D. in Educational Technology (University of Florida) and an AA in Spanish/Bilingual Studies (Foothill College), with a career spanning K-12 language teaching (including international contexts), instructional design for federal and corporate clients, and AI-powered education systems. He is currently pursuing a BA in Spanish at ENMU, giving him active learner perspective alongside scholarly authority.

4. **Addresses the full SLA pipeline** -- From input and intake through interaction, output, feedback, and assessment -- showing where AI intervenes at each stage and what the theoretical and empirical implications are.

5. **Balances advocacy with critique** -- Neither techno-utopian nor Luddite, the book applies a critical lens informed by equity, access, linguistic diversity, and the limits of current AI systems, while acknowledging genuine affordances.

## 4. Chapter Specifications

### Chapter 1: The Convergence -- Why AI and SLA Need Each Other

**Description**: Establishes the book's central argument: AI is not merely a new tool for language teaching but a development that fundamentally challenges and extends core SLA constructs. Traces the parallel histories of CALL (Computer-Assisted Language Learning) and AI/NLP, showing how they have converged in the era of large language models. Defines key terms and sets methodological expectations for the volume. Presents the book's organizing framework: AI interventions mapped onto the SLA processing pipeline (input, interaction, output, feedback, assessment, identity).

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Warschauer's (2004) CALL periodization (Structural, Communicative, Integrative); Chapelle's (2001) criteria for CALL evaluation; Garrett's (2009) technology-SLA integration model; historical overview of ELIZA, PLATO, and early NLP.

**Practical Applications**: Framework for evaluating AI language tools; decision matrix for program administrators; overview of the book's structure as a course syllabus.

**Estimated Word Count**: 8,000

---

### Chapter 2: Foundations of SLA Theory for the AI Age

**Description**: Provides a concise but rigorous review of the major SLA theories that will be referenced throughout the book. Each theory is presented in its canonical form and then examined for its compatibility with AI-mediated learning environments. This chapter ensures that readers from educational technology backgrounds have sufficient SLA grounding, and that readers from linguistics backgrounds see how established theories map onto computational contexts.

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Krashen's Monitor Model (Input Hypothesis, Affective Filter, Natural Order); Long's Interaction Hypothesis and focus on form; Swain's Output Hypothesis and pushed output; VanPatten's Input Processing and Processing Instruction; Schmidt's Noticing Hypothesis; Sociocultural Theory (Vygotsky, Lantolf) and the ZPD; Skill Acquisition Theory (DeKeyser); Usage-Based Approaches (Ellis, Tomasello); Complex Dynamic Systems Theory (Larsen-Freeman, de Bot).

**Practical Applications**: Theory-to-tool mapping table; self-assessment for readers to identify their theoretical orientation; discussion questions for graduate seminars.

**Estimated Word Count**: 10,000

---

### Chapter 3: How AI "Understands" Language -- NLP Fundamentals for SLA Researchers

**Description**: Demystifies natural language processing for a non-technical audience. Covers tokenization, embeddings, attention mechanisms, and the transformer architecture at a conceptual level. Explains how large language models (GPT, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA) generate and process text, and what this means for their capacity to serve as interlocutors, tutors, and assessors. Critically examines what LLMs actually "know" about language versus what they statistically approximate.

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Distributional semantics (Firth, Harris); Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017); scaling laws; RLHF and instruction tuning; Bender and Koller's (2020) "stochastic parrots" critique; Chomsky's competence/performance distinction as applied to LLMs.

**Practical Applications**: Guided exploration of an LLM's linguistic capabilities; prompt engineering basics for language educators; activity comparing LLM output to learner interlanguage.

**Estimated Word Count**: 9,000

---

### Chapter 4: AI-Enhanced Input -- Comprehensible, Personalized, and Infinite

**Description**: Examines how AI transforms the provision of linguistic input, the foundational construct in virtually all SLA theories. Covers AI-powered reading and listening tools that adapt difficulty level in real time (e.g., adaptive readers, AI-generated graded texts, text simplification algorithms). Evaluates these against Krashen's i+1 hypothesis, VanPatten's Input Processing theory, and the concept of enhanced input (Sharwood Smith). Analyzes whether AI-generated input qualifies as "authentic" and what implications arise from learners receiving input from non-human sources.

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Krashen's Input Hypothesis (i+1); VanPatten's Input Processing; Sharwood Smith's input enhancement; Nation's (2001) vocabulary load and graded readers; extensive reading research (Day & Bamford); multimodal input (Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning).

**Practical Applications**: Designing AI-powered extensive reading programs; using LLMs to generate level-appropriate texts; AI tools for captioning and subtitling (Whisper, YouTube auto-captions); case study of Duolingo's adaptive content engine.

**Estimated Word Count**: 9,000

---

### Chapter 5: Interaction in the Age of AI -- Negotiation of Meaning with Machines

**Description**: Investigates how AI chatbots and conversational agents fulfill (or fail to fulfill) the interactional conditions identified by Long, Pica, Varonis, and Gass as facilitative of acquisition. Examines whether AI-human conversation triggers negotiation of meaning, confirmation checks, comprehension checks, and recasts in ways comparable to human-human interaction. Introduces the concept of "synthetic interaction" and its theoretical status within the Interaction Hypothesis framework.

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Long's Interaction Hypothesis; Pica's (1994) conditions for negotiation; Varonis and Gass (1985) NNS-NNS interaction; Mackey's (1999) interactional feedback research; Gass and Mackey's (2015) interaction framework; Sociocultural Theory's concept of scaffolding and the ZPD applied to AI interlocutors.

**Practical Applications**: Designing chatbot conversation tasks; evaluating AI chatbot platforms (ChatGPT, Replika, Speak, TalkPal) for interactional quality; rubric for assessing whether AI conversations promote acquisition; sample task sequences pairing AI chatbot practice with human follow-up.

**Estimated Word Count**: 9,000

---

### Chapter 6: Output, Pushed Production, and the AI Feedback Loop

**Description**: Analyzes how AI tools support and shape learner output, a construct central to Swain's Output Hypothesis and Skill Acquisition Theory. Examines AI writing assistants (Grammarly, LanguageTool, LLM-based writing tutors), AI-powered speaking practice tools, and automated translation as a composing aid. Investigates whether AI scaffolding of output promotes the three functions Swain identified: noticing, hypothesis testing, and metalinguistic reflection. Addresses the paradox of AI making output "easier" while SLA theory values productive struggle.

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Swain's Output Hypothesis (noticing, hypothesis testing, metalinguistic functions); Skill Acquisition Theory (DeKeyser) and the role of practice; Levelt's (1989) speech production model; de Bot's (1992) bilingual production model; Skehan's (1998) trade-off hypothesis (complexity, accuracy, fluency).

**Practical Applications**: Task design that leverages AI feedback without eliminating productive struggle; using AI for controlled-to-free writing progressions; AI-mediated speaking tasks with delayed feedback; comparison of AI writing tools for L2 contexts.

**Estimated Word Count**: 9,000

---

### Chapter 7: Corrective Feedback -- AI as the Tireless Interlocutor

**Description**: Provides an in-depth treatment of AI-generated corrective feedback, arguably the most impactful current application of AI in language learning. Reviews the extensive SLA literature on corrective feedback types (recasts, prompts, explicit correction, metalinguistic explanation) and evaluates which types AI systems can deliver effectively. Examines the timing, explicitness, and individualization of AI feedback against research findings from Lyster and Ranta, Li, Mackey, and others.

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Lyster and Ranta's (1997) corrective feedback taxonomy; Li's (2010) meta-analysis of CF effectiveness; Mackey and Goo's (2007) interaction and feedback research; Schmidt's Noticing Hypothesis as applied to feedback salience; Bitchener and Ferris's (2012) written corrective feedback research; Sheen's (2011) oral CF studies.

**Practical Applications**: Configuring AI tools for optimal feedback types; comparing automated grammar feedback (Grammarly, Write & Improve, Criterion) against human tutor feedback; designing hybrid feedback systems; case study of Cambridge Write & Improve and ETS Criterion.

**Estimated Word Count**: 9,000

---

### Chapter 8: Pronunciation, Phonology, and AI Speech Systems

**Description**: Examines AI-powered pronunciation instruction and assessment through the lens of L2 phonology research. Covers automatic speech recognition (ASR) systems, text-to-speech (TTS) as input models, and AI pronunciation coaches. Evaluates the accuracy and pedagogical validity of ASR-based feedback for segmental and suprasegmental features. Addresses whose pronunciation norms AI systems encode and the implications for World Englishes and pluricentric language varieties.

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Flege's Speech Learning Model (SLM/SLM-r); Best's Perceptual Assimilation Model (PAM); Derwing and Munro's intelligibility/comprehensibility/accentedness distinction; Jenkins' Lingua Franca Core; Levis's (2005) nativeness vs. intelligibility principles; Automatic Speech Recognition error analysis.

**Practical Applications**: Evaluating pronunciation apps (ELSA Speak, Speechling, SpeechAce); designing ASR-assisted pronunciation curricula; using TTS for listening discrimination activities; addressing accent bias in AI systems; sample pronunciation teaching unit integrating AI tools.

**Estimated Word Count**: 8,500

---

### Chapter 9: Vocabulary Acquisition and AI -- From Flashcards to Contextual Intelligence

**Description**: Traces the evolution of AI-assisted vocabulary learning from simple spaced repetition systems to contextually aware, adaptive vocabulary engines. Examines how AI can operationalize key vocabulary acquisition constructs: depth of processing, incidental vs. intentional learning, the involvement load hypothesis, and Nation's four strands. Evaluates whether AI-generated example sentences and contextual definitions promote the kind of rich, elaborative processing that leads to long-term retention.

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Nation's (2001) four strands and vocabulary size thresholds; Laufer and Hulstijn's (2001) Involvement Load Hypothesis; Craik and Lockhart's Depth of Processing; Webb's (2007) receptive/productive knowledge dimensions; Schmitt's (2010) vocabulary acquisition research; spaced repetition algorithms (Leitner, SM-2, FSRS).

**Practical Applications**: Designing AI-enhanced vocabulary programs; comparative analysis of Anki (FSRS algorithm), Memrise, Quizlet AI, and LLM-based vocabulary generation; using AI for corpus-derived frequency lists and personalized word lists; activity templates for AI-assisted extensive reading with vocabulary tracking.

**Estimated Word Count**: 8,500

---

### Chapter 10: Grammar Instruction, Focus on Form, and AI

**Description**: Analyzes how AI mediates grammar instruction across the implicit/explicit continuum. Examines AI-powered grammar checkers, adaptive grammar exercises, and LLM-based explanations through the lens of focus-on-form research, Processing Instruction, and the interface debate. Investigates whether AI can deliver the kind of reactive, meaning-contingent focus on form that Long advocated, or whether it defaults to focus-on-formS (discrete-point grammar teaching).

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Long's focus on form vs. focus on forms distinction; VanPatten's Processing Instruction; Ellis's (2006) principles of instructed SLA; DeKeyser's Skill Acquisition Theory (declarative to procedural knowledge); Pienemann's Processability Theory and teachability; the interface debate (strong, weak, no-interface positions).

**Practical Applications**: Using AI for input flood and input enhancement activities; designing Processing Instruction tasks with AI-generated structured input; AI-adaptive grammar exercise platforms; comparison of grammar teaching approaches across AI tools (Duolingo, Babbel, Busuu, LLM tutors).

**Estimated Word Count**: 8,500

### Chapter 11: AI-Powered Language Assessment and Adaptive Testing

**Description**: Critically examines automated language assessment, from established systems (TOEFL iBT automated scoring, Pearson's Versant) to emergent LLM-based assessment. Addresses construct validity, reliability, washback, and fairness in AI scoring. Evaluates computer-adaptive testing (CAT) for placement, diagnostic, and achievement purposes. Discusses the tension between efficiency gains and the risk of construct underrepresentation when AI scores complex language performances.

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Bachman and Palmer's (2010) language assessment framework; construct validity (Messick, 1989); Chapelle, Enright, and Jamieson's (2008) TOEFL validity argument; Item Response Theory and CAT; automated essay scoring research (Shermis & Burstein); Xi's (2010) automated speaking assessment; washback (Alderson & Wall, 1993).

**Practical Applications**: Evaluating AI-scored assessments for program-level decisions; designing hybrid human-AI assessment systems; using AI for formative assessment and learning analytics; case studies of TOEFL iBT, Duolingo English Test, and Pearson Versant; ethical guidelines for AI assessment adoption.

**Estimated Word Count**: 9,000

### Chapter 12: Sociocultural Theory, Identity, and AI-Mediated Language Learning

**Description**: Explores the social, cultural, and identity dimensions of learning language through and with AI systems. Draws on Sociocultural Theory, language socialization research, and identity theories (Norton, Darvin) to examine how AI mediates learner agency, investment, and imagined communities. Investigates critical questions: Does conversing with AI constitute legitimate social interaction? How do power dynamics shift when the interlocutor is an algorithm? What happens to language learner identity when AI removes the social risk of error?

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory; Lantolf and Thorne's (2006) SCT and L2; Norton's (2013) identity and investment; Darvin and Norton's (2015) model of investment; Duff's (2019) language socialization; Kramsch's (2009) multilingual subject; Thorne's (2003) mediation and artifacts; Warschauer's (2003) digital divide.

**Practical Applications**: Designing AI-augmented collaborative tasks that preserve social interaction; using AI to create safe spaces for identity exploration in L2; addressing equity and access in AI-mediated language programs; case study of AI-assisted heritage language maintenance.

**Estimated Word Count**: 9,000

### Chapter 13: AI and Multilingualism -- Beyond the Monolingual Bias

**Description**: Challenges the predominantly English-centric and monolingual-norm orientation of both AI systems and SLA research. Examines AI capabilities across typologically diverse languages, including less-resourced languages. Discusses translanguaging, plurilingualism, and the role of AI in supporting (or undermining) multilingual repertoires. Evaluates machine translation as a pedagogical tool rather than a threat, drawing on emerging research on translation as a cognitive and communicative activity.

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Cook's (2001) multi-competence; Garcia and Wei's (2014) translanguaging; Cenoz and Gorter's (2015) multilingual education; Canagarajah's (2013) translingual practice; Kern's (2014) literacy and technology; Gonzalez-Davies' (2004) translation in language teaching; low-resource NLP research (Joshi et al., 2020).

**Practical Applications**: Using AI translation tools as pedagogical scaffolds; AI for heritage language and indigenous language revitalization; designing multilingual AI-assisted curricula; evaluating AI performance across language families; case study of AI-assisted Zapotec language documentation.

**Estimated Word Count**: 9,000

### Chapter 14: Research Methods for AI-SLA Studies

**Description**: Provides methodological guidance for researchers investigating AI in SLA contexts. Covers experimental, quasi-experimental, mixed-methods, and design-based research approaches appropriate for studying AI-mediated language learning. Addresses unique challenges: controlling for AI variability, measuring learning gains when AI personalizes input, ethical considerations in AI research with human subjects, and the reproducibility problem when commercial AI systems update frequently.

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Plonsky and Gass (2011) quantitative research in SLA; Duff (2008) case study research in applied linguistics; Design-Based Research (Collins, Joseph, & Bielaczyc, 2004); Chapelle's (2001) CALL evaluation criteria adapted for AI; ethical frameworks for AI research (Jobin, Ienca, & Vayena, 2019); IRIS database and open science in SLA.

**Practical Applications**: Research design templates for AI-SLA studies; sample IRB protocols; tools for logging and analyzing AI-learner interactions; statistical approaches for AI-adaptive environments; guide to pre-registration and open materials for AI-SLA research.

**Estimated Word Count**: 9,000

### Chapter 15: The Road Ahead -- Agentic AI, Embodied Interaction, and the Future of Language Learning

**Description**: Synthesizes the book's arguments and projects forward. Examines emerging technologies -- agentic AI systems that plan and execute multi-step learning sequences, embodied AI in AR/VR environments, multimodal AI that integrates text, speech, vision, and gesture, and brain-computer interfaces -- through SLA theoretical lenses. Proposes a research agenda for the next decade. Concludes with a principled framework for evaluating future AI language learning technologies against SLA theory and humanistic values.

**Key Theories/Frameworks**: Atkinson's (2011) sociocognitive approach; Thorne and Hellermann's (2015) embodied interaction; Sykes and Reinhardt's (2013) game-mediated L2; Chapelle's evaluation framework extended for agentic AI; transhumanism and posthumanism in applied linguistics (Pennycook, 2018); Douglas Fir Group's (2016) transdisciplinary framework.

**Practical Applications**: Evaluating AR/VR language learning platforms; designing for agentic AI tutoring systems; future-proofing language programs against rapid technological change; a 10-question rubric for evaluating any future AI language learning tool.

**Estimated Word Count**: 8,500

## 9. Sample Chapter Outline -- Chapter 1: The Convergence

### Chapter 1: The Convergence -- Why AI and SLA Need Each Other

**Target Length**: 8,000 words

---

#### 1.1 Opening Vignette (500 words)

A narrative opening: A language learner in 2026 wakes up, practices speaking with an AI tutor over breakfast, reads an AI-adapted news article in their L2 during their commute, receives AI-generated corrective feedback on a writing assignment at lunch, and reviews AI-curated vocabulary before bed. Contrast with the same learner's experience in 2006 and 1996. The question: Is this learner acquiring language? What would Krashen, Long, Swain, and Vygotsky say?

#### 1.2 Defining the Territory (1,200 words)

- 1.2.1 What counts as "AI" in this book -- operational definitions distinguishing rule-based systems, statistical NLP, machine learning, deep learning, and large language models
- 1.2.2 What counts as "SLA" -- acquisition vs. learning; naturalistic vs. instructed; L2 vs. Ln; heritage languages; the scope of the field
- 1.2.3 The CALL-to-AI transition -- why "CALL" as a label is insufficient for the current moment and why a new synthesis is needed

#### 1.3 A Brief History of Technology in Language Learning (1,500 words)

- 1.3.1 The language laboratory era (1950s-1970s) -- audiolingual method and behaviorist technology
- 1.3.2 Mainframe and early PC CALL (1970s-1990s) -- PLATO, text reconstruction, early parsers
- 1.3.3 Warschauer's three phases: Structural CALL, Communicative CALL, Integrative CALL
- 1.3.4 The internet era and CMC (1990s-2010s) -- email exchanges, tandem learning, MOOs, social media
- 1.3.5 Mobile-Assisted Language Learning and gamification (2010s) -- Duolingo, Babbel, Memrise
- 1.3.6 The LLM inflection point (2022-present) -- ChatGPT, voice AI, multimodal systems

#### 1.4 A Brief History of AI in Language (1,200 words)

- 1.4.1 ELIZA and the illusion of understanding (1966)
- 1.4.2 Rule-based MT and its failures (1950s-1990s)
- 1.4.3 Statistical NLP and the corpus revolution (1990s-2010s)
- 1.4.4 Deep learning breakthroughs: word2vec, sequence-to-sequence, attention
- 1.4.5 The transformer (2017) and the scaling paradigm
- 1.4.6 GPT, BERT, and the emergence of general-purpose language AI (2018-2026)

#### 1.5 The Central Argument: AI as a Theoretical Provocation (1,500 words)

- 1.5.1 AI does not merely implement SLA theory -- it challenges core constructs
- 1.5.2 What happens to "input" when it is infinite and algorithmically personalized?
- 1.5.3 What happens to "interaction" when the interlocutor has no communicative intent?
- 1.5.4 What happens to "output" when AI can produce it for you?
- 1.5.5 What happens to "identity" when the social stakes of error are removed?
- 1.5.6 These are not merely practical questions -- they are theoretical ones that advance the field

#### 1.6 The Organizing Framework: AI Across the SLA Pipeline (1,000 words)

- 1.6.1 The processing pipeline: Input --> Intake --> Interaction --> Output --> Feedback --> Assessment
