# Chapter 6: Output, Pushed Production, and the AI Feedback Loop

---

## 6.1 Introduction: When the Machine Writes for You

In the summer of 2023, a language teacher at a Japanese university noticed something unusual in her intermediate English writing students' essays. The sentences were grammatically flawless, the vocabulary was sophisticated, and the arguments were well-organized. But the voice was wrong. Every essay sounded the same -- polished, neutral, and devoid of the charming imperfections that characterize developing interlanguage. Further investigation revealed that approximately one-third of the class had used ChatGPT to write or substantially revise their essays before submission. The students were not trying to cheat; they were trying to produce "good English." The AI had given them exactly that. What it had not given them was the opportunity to struggle with the language, to notice gaps in their knowledge, to test hypotheses about English syntax, or to develop the productive competence that would serve them in any context where ChatGPT was not available.

This anecdote, repeated with variations in language classrooms around the world since late 2022, encapsulates the central paradox that this chapter addresses: AI systems that can produce language *for* learners may fundamentally undermine the process by which learners develop the ability to produce language *themselves*. The paradox is not merely practical (how do we prevent cheating?) but deeply theoretical. If Swain's (1985, 1995) Output Hypothesis is correct -- if the act of producing language serves cognitive functions that are distinct from and complementary to the processing of input -- then AI tools that reduce or eliminate the need for learners to produce language independently may remove a critical mechanism of acquisition.

This chapter examines the intersection of AI and learner output through six major sections. First, it revisits the theoretical foundations of output in SLA, establishing the specific claims about what output does for acquisition that AI tools must be evaluated against. Second, it analyzes AI writing assistants and their impact on L2 writing development. Third, it examines AI tools for speech production and pronunciation. Fourth, it confronts the paradox directly: the tension between AI systems that produce output for learners and the pedagogical imperative to push learners to produce output themselves. Fifth, it explores the balance between scaffolding and productive struggle in AI-mediated production tasks. Finally, it reviews the research evidence on AI-assisted versus AI-dependent output and proposes guidelines for practice.

## 6.2 Theoretical Foundations: What Output Does for Acquisition

### 6.2.1 Swain's Output Hypothesis

Merrill Swain proposed the Output Hypothesis in 1985, initially as a corrective to Krashen's claim that comprehensible input alone was sufficient for acquisition. Swain's argument, grounded in her observations of French immersion students in Canada, was that these students -- who received abundant comprehensible input in French throughout their schooling -- had developed strong receptive skills but remained far weaker in productive skills, particularly in grammatical accuracy and sociolinguistic appropriateness. If input were sufficient, these students should have been native-like producers of French. They were not.

Swain (1995, 2005) subsequently elaborated the Output Hypothesis by identifying three specific functions that production serves in the acquisition process:

**1. The noticing/triggering function.** When learners attempt to produce language, they become aware of gaps between what they want to say and what they are able to say. This awareness -- the experience of reaching for a word or structure and finding that one does not have it -- triggers noticing of the relevant forms in subsequent input. In Schmidt's (1990) terms, output makes learners "notice the gap" between their interlanguage and the target language, which in turn drives selective attention to those forms in subsequent input processing. Production, in this account, is not merely the result of acquisition; it is a catalyst for further acquisition.

**2. The hypothesis-testing function.** Production allows learners to test hypotheses about how the target language works. When a learner produces an utterance, they are in effect positing a hypothesis about the grammatical, lexical, or pragmatic acceptability of that form. If the utterance is understood and accepted, the hypothesis is (provisionally) confirmed. If it triggers confusion or corrective feedback, the hypothesis is disconfirmed, and the learner has evidence for revision. This trial-and-error process is central to the development of interlanguage and cannot occur through receptive processing alone.

**3. The metalinguistic/reflective function.** Production, particularly in collaborative contexts, can prompt learners to reflect on language form, to discuss and analyze linguistic structure, and to develop explicit metalinguistic knowledge. Swain and Lapkin (1995) documented what they called "language-related episodes" (LREs) in collaborative writing tasks -- moments when learners paused to discuss language form, consider alternatives, and make conscious decisions about linguistic choices. These episodes represent a form of language learning that is unique to production and that contributes to both explicit knowledge and, potentially, to the proceduralization of that knowledge for subsequent automatic production.

### 6.2.2 The Concept of Pushed Output

Central to Swain's argument is the concept of *pushed output* -- production that stretches learners beyond their current comfort zone, requiring them to deploy linguistic resources at the edge of their competence. The term "pushed" is critical: it implies that the learner is being challenged to produce language that is more complex, more accurate, or more sociolinguistically appropriate than what they would produce if left to their own communicative devices. In task-based language teaching, this push comes from task demands (e.g., a narrative retelling task that requires past tense forms, a persuasive essay that requires conditional structures) and from interlocutor pressure (e.g., a conversation partner who requests clarification, demanding greater precision).

The concept of pushed output creates a direct tension with AI systems. AI writing assistants "push" in the opposite direction: they push the learner's output *upward*, making it more accurate and sophisticated through real-time correction, suggestion, and completion. But this is not the kind of push that Swain theorized. Swain's pushed output requires the *learner* to do the pushing -- to struggle with form, to reach beyond current competence, to experience the productive difficulty that triggers noticing and hypothesis testing. When AI does the pushing on the learner's behalf, the cognitive processes that Swain identified as facilitative of acquisition may not be engaged.

### 6.2.3 Skill Acquisition Theory and the Role of Practice

DeKeyser's (1998, 2007) Skill Acquisition Theory (SAT), drawing on Anderson's (1993) ACT-R model of cognitive skill development, provides another theoretical lens for evaluating AI's impact on output. SAT posits that language competence develops through three stages: *declarative knowledge* (knowing rules and facts about the language), *proceduralization* (the conversion of declarative knowledge into production procedures through practice), and *automatization* (the development of fluent, effortless performance through extensive practice). Each stage requires the learner to *do* something: to study rules, to practice applying them in production, and to practice again and again until performance becomes automatic.

AI tools that produce or substantially revise language on the learner's behalf potentially short-circuit this developmental sequence. A learner who knows the rule for the Spanish subjunctive (declarative knowledge) but uses ChatGPT to write sentences requiring the subjunctive (thereby bypassing proceduralization) will not develop the ability to produce the subjunctive independently. The AI has provided the correct output, but the learner has not engaged in the practice that SAT identifies as necessary for proceduralizing and automatizing the underlying skill.

### 6.2.4 Levelt's Speech Production Model

Levelt's (1989) model of speech production, adapted for bilingual speakers by de Bot (1992), describes speaking as a multi-stage process involving conceptualization (deciding what to say), formulation (encoding the message linguistically through grammatical encoding, morphophonological encoding, and phonetic encoding), and articulation (the motor execution of speech). Each stage involves cognitive processing that contributes to the development of productive competence.

AI voice assistants and text-to-speech systems can perform the formulation and articulation stages on the learner's behalf, allowing the learner to conceptualize a message in their L1 or in simplified L2 and have the AI produce the well-formulated, accurately articulated output. This may be useful as a modeling tool -- hearing how one's intended message should sound in the L2 -- but it is not productive practice in Levelt's sense, because the learner has not engaged in the formulation processes that develop L2 productive competence.

### 6.2.5 Skehan's Trade-Off Hypothesis

Skehan (1998) proposed that learners operating under real-time processing constraints face trade-offs among three dimensions of production: *complexity* (the range and sophistication of linguistic structures used), *accuracy* (the degree of conformity to target language norms), and *fluency* (the speed and smoothness of production). Learners have limited attentional resources, and attending to one dimension typically comes at the cost of another. This trade-off is a normal and arguably necessary feature of language development: the experience of trying to produce complex, accurate, and fluent language simultaneously is what drives the development of the processing efficiency that eventually allows all three dimensions to be managed concurrently.

AI tools disrupt these trade-offs in theoretically significant ways. A writing assistant that corrects errors in real time eliminates the accuracy burden, freeing the learner to focus on complexity and fluency. A speech-to-text system that transcribes the learner's speech and then corrects it eliminates both the fluency and accuracy burdens. While this redistribution of cognitive resources may feel productive to the learner, it removes the processing challenge that Skehan's model suggests drives the development of integrated production ability.

## 6.3 AI Writing Assistants and L2 Writing Development

### 6.3.1 The Landscape of AI Writing Tools

The landscape of AI writing tools available to language learners has expanded dramatically since 2020. These tools can be categorized along a continuum from minimal intervention to maximal production:

**Grammar and style checkers (minimal intervention).** Tools like Grammarly, LanguageTool, ProWritingAid, and Microsoft Editor identify grammatical errors, spelling mistakes, and style issues and suggest corrections. The learner remains the primary producer of text; the tool functions as an automated proofreader. Grammarly, the market leader, claims over 30 million daily active users and uses a combination of rule-based NLP and machine learning classifiers to identify errors across a wide range of categories (Grammarly, 2024).

**Writing enhancement tools (moderate intervention).** Tools like QuillBot, Wordtune, and Hemingway Editor go beyond error correction to suggest alternative phrasings, simplify complex sentences, change register, and improve clarity. These tools do not write for the learner but substantially transform the learner's output, potentially replacing the learner's authentic voice with a polished approximation.

**AI co-writers and generators (maximal production).** ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and other LLMs can generate complete texts from prompts, continue and complete partially written texts, and substantially rewrite existing texts. A learner who types "Write a paragraph about my hometown in Spanish" receives a complete, grammatically correct paragraph that they did not write. A learner who types their own paragraph and then asks "Can you improve this?" receives a revised version that may bear little resemblance to their original production.

### 6.3.2 Grammar Checkers and Error Correction: The Evidence

Research on automated grammar checkers in L2 writing has produced a nuanced picture. On the positive side, studies have shown that grammar checkers can help learners identify and correct certain types of surface-level errors, particularly those involving subject-verb agreement, article usage, and spelling (Burstein, Chodorow, & Leacock, 2004; Ranalli, 2018). Learners who use grammar checkers consistently report increased confidence in their writing and greater awareness of their common error patterns (Li, Link, & Hegelheimer, 2015).

However, the research also reveals significant limitations. O'Regan, Rivens Mompean, and Desmet (2020) found that L2 writers using Grammarly corrected more surface-level errors but did not show improvement in the complexity or organization of their writing. The tool directed attention toward accuracy at the sentence level while providing no support for the higher-order discourse and rhetorical skills that distinguish proficient from developing writers. Ranalli (2018) found that L2 writers accepted Grammarly's suggestions uncritically in many cases, without understanding why the change was necessary or developing the ability to identify similar errors independently. This pattern -- accepting corrections without processing them cognitively -- is precisely the kind of shallow engagement that fails to produce durable learning.

Koltovskaia (2020) conducted a particularly revealing study comparing L2 writers using Grammarly with those receiving human feedback on their errors. The Grammarly group corrected more errors during the revision process but showed no greater improvement on subsequent writing tasks, while the human feedback group showed significant improvement. The difference, Koltovskaia argued, was that human feedback required the learner to notice the error, understand the explanation, and produce the correction independently, while Grammarly's automated corrections could be accepted with a single click, bypassing the cognitive processing that leads to learning.

### 6.3.3 AI Generators and the Death of Productive Struggle

The use of LLMs to generate or substantially revise L2 writing represents a qualitatively different challenge from grammar checkers. When a learner uses ChatGPT to write an essay, the three functions of output that Swain identified are entirely bypassed:

**Noticing is eliminated.** The learner does not experience gaps between what they want to say and what they can say, because the AI says it for them. The metacognitive awareness that drives selective attention to subsequent input is not triggered.

**Hypothesis testing is eliminated.** The learner does not test any hypotheses about L2 forms, because they do not produce any L2 forms. The trial-and-error process through which interlanguage develops does not occur.

**Metalinguistic reflection may be eliminated or may occur.** If the learner simply submits the AI-generated text without reading it carefully, metalinguistic reflection does not occur. If the learner reads the AI-generated text and compares it with what they would have written, some reflection may occur -- but this is a receptive process (reading and comparing) rather than a productive one (writing and revising).

The theoretical prediction, then, is that sustained reliance on AI text generation will impede the development of productive competence, even if the submitted texts are of high quality. Several early empirical studies support this prediction. Yan (2023) found that Chinese EFL students who used ChatGPT extensively for writing assignments showed declining performance on in-class writing tasks completed without AI assistance over a semester, while students who used AI minimally or not at all showed improvement. The AI-using students had effectively outsourced the practice that builds writing competence, and their competence had atrophied as a result.

Escalante, Pack, and Barrett (2023) studied Japanese university students' writing processes with and without AI assistance and found that students using AI spent significantly less time on planning and formulation -- the cognitively demanding stages of writing that Bereiter and Scardamalia (1987) identified as the engines of writing development. The AI students spent their time primarily on prompting and selecting from AI-generated alternatives, which is a qualitatively different cognitive activity from composing.

### 6.3.4 The Spectrum of AI Integration in Writing

Not all AI use in writing is equally detrimental to productive development. A more nuanced analysis recognizes a spectrum of integration that has different implications for the functions of output:

**AI as post-writing proofreader.** The learner writes independently, then uses an AI tool to identify errors in the completed draft. The learner has engaged in the full production process -- noticing gaps, testing hypotheses, making choices -- before the AI intervenes. The AI's role is limited to error identification, and the learner must still decide how to correct each error. This integration preserves most of the productive functions of output.

**AI as during-writing assistant.** The learner writes with a grammar checker or style tool active in real time, receiving corrections and suggestions as they compose. The production process is modified: some gaps are noticed by the AI before the learner notices them, some hypotheses are tested and corrected before the learner has the opportunity to evaluate them. The productive functions are partially preserved but partially pre-empted.

**AI as pre-writing generator.** The learner prompts the AI to generate text that they then revise, adapt, or adopt. The production process is substantially outsourced: the learner did not compose the text, and their role is limited to editing and accepting. The productive functions are largely eliminated, though some metalinguistic reflection may occur during the editing process.

**AI as ghostwriter.** The learner submits AI-generated text with minimal or no modification. The productive functions are entirely eliminated. The learner has produced nothing and has not engaged any of the cognitive processes that output is theorized to serve.

These categories are not fixed; a learner may move between them across tasks and over time. The pedagogical challenge is to guide learners toward the more productive end of the spectrum -- using AI in ways that support and extend their own production rather than replacing it.

## 6.4 Speech Production and AI Pronunciation Tools

### 6.4.1 AI and Speaking Practice

Speech production presents different challenges and opportunities from writing, because speaking occurs in real time with limited opportunity for deliberate planning and revision. Levelt's (1989) model emphasizes the automaticity requirements of speech production: unlike writing, where the learner can pause, reconsider, and revise, speaking requires rapid, fluent formulation and articulation. The development of speaking proficiency therefore depends heavily on practice -- the repeated, effortful production of speech that builds the procedural knowledge and processing speed necessary for real-time communication.

AI speaking practice tools take several forms. Voice-enabled chatbots (Speak, TalkPal) provide conversational practice with ASR-based recognition and LLM-generated responses. Pronunciation coaches (ELSA Speak, Speechling, SpeechAce) focus specifically on phonological accuracy, using ASR to analyze learner pronunciation and provide targeted feedback. Shadowing and repetition tools allow learners to listen to model speech and practice producing it, with AI providing feedback on accuracy.

### 6.4.2 ASR-Based Pronunciation Feedback

Automatic speech recognition systems can provide immediate feedback on learner pronunciation, comparing the learner's speech to a target model and identifying segments that deviate from the expected pronunciation. The best current systems (ELSA Speak, SpeechAce) provide phoneme-level feedback, showing the learner which specific sounds they produced incorrectly and demonstrating the target pronunciation through audio and visual displays (spectrograms, sagittal diagrams, animated articulatory models).

The research on ASR-based pronunciation feedback is growing but mixed. Liakin, Cardoso, and Liakina (2015) found that learners using ASR-based pronunciation tools showed greater improvement in the production of targeted French vowels than a control group receiving traditional pronunciation instruction. Fouz-Gonzalez (2019) found similar positive results for English pronunciation among Spanish L1 learners using mobile ASR tools. However, both studies focused on segmental features (individual sounds) and did not examine suprasegmental features (intonation, stress, rhythm), which are arguably more important for intelligibility and communicative effectiveness (Derwing & Munro, 2005) but more difficult for ASR systems to assess.

A significant limitation of ASR-based pronunciation feedback is the *recognition threshold* problem. ASR systems are designed to recognize speech, not to evaluate it. They must decide whether a learner's utterance matches the expected input, and this binary decision (recognized/not recognized) provides limited pedagogical information. A learner whose pronunciation is slightly off may receive recognition (positive feedback for a marginally acceptable production), while a learner whose pronunciation is acceptable but includes unexpected prosodic variation may fail to be recognized (negative feedback for an adequate production). The mismatch between ASR recognition thresholds and pedagogical evaluation criteria is a persistent challenge in AI pronunciation feedback (Neri, Cucchiarini, Strik, & Boves, 2002).

### 6.4.3 The Accent Bias Problem

Whose pronunciation does the AI evaluate against? This question, discussed in greater depth in Chapter 8, has direct implications for the output function of speaking practice. If the AI's target model is Standard American English (the default for most English ASR systems), then learners who speak or are developing non-standard varieties -- Indian English, Nigerian English, Jamaican English, or any variety that diverges from the training data -- may receive negative feedback on productions that are perfectly intelligible and communicatively effective. This encoding of standard language ideology in AI pronunciation tools raises equity concerns (Koenecke et al., 2020) and may discourage learners from developing authentic productive competence in favor of conformity to an AI-imposed norm.

Levis's (2005) distinction between the *nativeness principle* (the goal is to sound like a native speaker) and the *intelligibility principle* (the goal is to be understood) is critical here. Most AI pronunciation tools implicitly adopt the nativeness principle, measuring learner production against a standard native speaker model. The intelligibility principle, which is increasingly favored in applied linguistics and TESOL (Derwing & Munro, 2015), would evaluate learner production by whether it is understood by a range of listeners, including non-native listeners. AI systems are not yet capable of evaluating intelligibility in this more nuanced sense, which means they may push learners toward an unrealistic and ideologically problematic pronunciation target.

### 6.4.4 AI-Mediated Speaking Tasks

Beyond pronunciation feedback, AI can be used to structure speaking tasks that push learners to produce extended, meaningful output. Role-play scenarios (e.g., a job interview simulation, a medical consultation, a debate) require the learner to formulate and articulate complex messages in real time, exercising the full range of Levelt's production processes. The AI's role in these tasks is to provide the interlocutor's turns, creating the context in which the learner must produce output.

The quality of these tasks depends heavily on whether the AI creates genuine communicative pressure. A role-play in which the AI accepts any learner response and moves to the next topic does not push the learner to produce better output. A role-play in which the AI signals non-understanding, asks for clarification, or introduces unexpected complications (e.g., the "customer" changes their mind, the "interviewer" asks a follow-up question) creates the conditions under which pushed output occurs. The design of AI speaking tasks is therefore at least as important as the AI technology itself; a well-designed task with a simple AI interlocutor may be more effective than a poorly designed task with a sophisticated one.

## 6.5 The Central Paradox: AI That Produces vs. AI That Pushes

### 6.5.1 Defining the Paradox

The central paradox of AI and learner output can be stated simply: the same AI capabilities that make AI useful for language production also make it possible to avoid language production entirely. An AI that can correct errors can also be used to avoid making errors. An AI that can suggest better phrasings can also be used to avoid generating one's own phrasings. An AI that can translate can also be used to avoid formulating in the L2 at all.

This paradox is not unique to AI; it has precedents in the history of language technology. The calculator raised similar concerns about mathematical learning: if the calculator can do the computation, will students learn to compute? The spell checker raised similar concerns about spelling: if the software corrects errors automatically, will writers learn to spell? In both cases, the empirical answer has been nuanced: technology can be used in ways that support or undermine skill development, depending on how it is integrated into the learning process and what expectations are maintained for independent performance.

The AI version of this paradox is more acute, however, because the range of linguistic tasks that AI can perform is vastly greater than what previous technologies could handle. A calculator computes; it does not write mathematical proofs. A spell checker corrects spelling; it does not compose paragraphs. An LLM can do virtually anything that involves language production, which means that virtually any productive language learning activity can be outsourced to AI. The scope of the threat to productive development is therefore broader and deeper than anything the field has previously encountered.

### 6.5.2 The Outsourcing Gradient

It is useful to conceptualize the paradox not as a binary (learner produces / AI produces) but as a gradient of cognitive outsourcing. At one end of the gradient, the learner does all the cognitive work and uses AI only for peripheral support (e.g., looking up a word in an AI-powered dictionary). At the other end, the AI does all the cognitive work and the learner merely prompts and accepts (e.g., asking ChatGPT to write an essay). In between lies a range of intermediate positions:

- The learner writes a sentence and asks the AI whether it is correct (cognitive work: formulation; outsourced: monitoring).
- The learner writes a paragraph and asks the AI to suggest improvements (cognitive work: initial formulation; outsourced: revision).
- The learner provides an outline and asks the AI to expand it into prose (cognitive work: conceptualization and organization; outsourced: formulation).
- The learner describes in their L1 what they want to say and asks the AI to produce it in the L2 (cognitive work: conceptualization; outsourced: formulation, translation).
- The learner asks the AI to generate a text on a given topic (cognitive work: topic selection; outsourced: everything else).

Each position on this gradient preserves some cognitive functions and outsources others. The pedagogical question is not "Should learners use AI?" but "At what position on this gradient should learners be working for any given task, and how should that position shift as proficiency develops?"

### 6.5.3 Machine Translation as a Production Tool

Machine translation (MT) deserves special attention within the output paradox because it represents the most direct form of production outsourcing: the learner thinks in their L1, and the AI produces the L2 output. The research on MT use by L2 writers has grown substantially since the release of neural machine translation systems (Google Translate NMT, DeepL) that produce significantly more fluent output than their statistical predecessors.

Tsai (2019) found that Taiwanese EFL students who used Google Translate as a composing aid produced texts with fewer errors and greater lexical diversity than students who composed without MT support, but that the MT-using students showed no greater improvement in subsequent unassisted writing. Lee (2020) found that Korean EFL students used MT primarily for vocabulary lookup and sentence-level translation rather than for wholesale text generation, suggesting that MT use exists on the outsourcing gradient rather than at its extreme end. Jolley and Maimone (2022) found that L2 French students used DeepL in a variety of ways, from checking individual words to generating entire passages, and that the degree of outsourcing correlated negatively with gains on subsequent writing assessments.

The theoretical concern with MT as a production tool is that it allows learners to bypass the formulation stage of production entirely. In Levelt's (1989) model, formulation is where the learner's grammatical knowledge is exercised and developed: selecting lexical items, building syntactic frames, applying morphological rules, and encoding phonological forms. When MT performs formulation, the learner's grammatical encoding processes are not engaged, and the practice that SAT identifies as necessary for proceduralization does not occur.

However, some scholars have argued for a more constructive view of MT use. Niño (2009) proposed that MT output can serve as a "draft" that learners revise and improve, engaging in the same error detection and correction processes that they would apply to their own writing. O'Brien (2012) argued that "post-editing" -- the revision of MT output to improve accuracy and fluency -- is itself a cognitively demanding task that requires linguistic knowledge and metalinguistic awareness. If learners are taught to use MT output critically rather than accepting it uncritically, the post-editing process may engage some of the cognitive functions that direct production would engage.

## 6.6 Balancing Scaffolding with Productive Struggle

### 6.6.1 The Concept of Desirable Difficulty

The tension between AI scaffolding and productive struggle maps onto a broader concept in learning science: *desirable difficulty* (Bjork, 1994; Bjork & Bjork, 2011). Desirable difficulties are conditions of learning that make the learning process more effortful and slower in the short term but produce more durable and transferable knowledge in the long term. Examples include spaced practice (distributing practice over time rather than massing it), interleaved practice (mixing different types of problems rather than blocking them), retrieval practice (testing oneself rather than re-reading), and generation (producing answers rather than recognizing them).

AI writing and speaking tools, by reducing the difficulty of production, may eliminate desirable difficulties that are essential for long-term productive development. A learner who uses Grammarly to catch all their errors in real time is not engaging in the effortful self-monitoring that builds error detection skills. A learner who uses ChatGPT to generate sentences they cannot produce independently is not engaging in the effortful retrieval of linguistic knowledge that strengthens memory traces. The short-term benefit (a better text, a more fluent utterance) comes at the cost of long-term development (the ability to produce quality text or fluent utterances independently).

The concept of desirable difficulty does not mean that all difficulty is desirable. Unproductive struggle -- attempting a task that is genuinely beyond the learner's current capability, with no support and no path forward -- is frustrating and demoralizing rather than facilitative. The art of effective language pedagogy is to calibrate the difficulty of productive tasks so that learners are stretched but not overwhelmed -- pushed to the edge of their competence, where the noticing, hypothesis testing, and metalinguistic reflection that Swain identified can occur.

AI can potentially serve this calibration function, but only if it is designed to *modulate* difficulty rather than eliminate it. An AI writing assistant that highlights errors without correcting them, that asks questions about the learner's intended meaning without providing the answer, or that offers multiple alternatives and asks the learner to choose and justify their choice is supporting productive struggle. An AI assistant that corrects errors automatically, generates polished prose on demand, or translates the learner's L1 thoughts into L2 text is eliminating productive struggle.

### 6.6.2 The Goldilocks Zone of AI Scaffolding

Drawing on Vygotsky's ZPD and Wood et al.'s (1976) scaffolding framework, we can conceptualize the ideal AI scaffolding for output as occupying a "Goldilocks zone" between too much support (which eliminates productive struggle) and too little support (which produces unproductive frustration):

**Too much support (AI dependency zone):**
- AI generates or substantially rewrites the learner's text
- AI corrects all errors automatically without learner engagement
- AI provides the answer whenever the learner hesitates
- Learner's role is reduced to prompting and accepting

**Goldilocks zone (productive scaffolding):**
- AI identifies errors but requires the learner to generate corrections
- AI provides targeted hints when the learner is stuck, not complete solutions
- AI asks questions that guide the learner's thinking ("What tense do you need here?")
- AI provides models that the learner adapts rather than copies
- AI adjusts the level of support based on the learner's demonstrated ability

**Too little support (frustration zone):**
- No AI assistance available
- Learner struggles with tasks that are genuinely beyond current capability
- Errors accumulate without feedback
- Learner disengages or resorts to avoidance strategies (simplification, topic avoidance)

The challenge for AI system designers is that the Goldilocks zone is not a fixed location; it shifts with the learner's proficiency, the difficulty of the task, the linguistic features involved, and the learner's motivational state. A novice writer may need more AI support on a complex essay than an intermediate writer does on a simple one. The same learner may need more support on morphologically complex structures than on word order. Adaptive AI scaffolding that adjusts the level and type of support in response to the learner's ongoing performance is the theoretical ideal, but implementing it requires the kind of dynamic, contingent responsiveness that, as Chapter 5 argued, remains beyond the capabilities of current systems.

### 6.6.3 Graduated Scaffolding: A Developmental Approach

A developmental approach to AI scaffolding would adjust the position on the outsourcing gradient as the learner's proficiency develops. Beginning learners, who lack the productive resources to generate extended L2 output independently, might legitimately use more AI support -- sentence starters, vocabulary suggestions, model texts to adapt. As proficiency develops, AI support should be systematically withdrawn, pushing the learner toward increasingly independent production. By advanced levels, the learner should be producing extended output independently, using AI only for post-production proofreading or for checking specific questions about usage.

This graduated approach mirrors the scaffolding sequence that effective human tutors employ: heavy support initially, with progressive withdrawal as the learner internalizes the scaffolded performance. The difference is that human tutors make moment-to-moment judgments about when to provide support and when to withdraw it, based on rich evidence of the learner's cognitive and affective state. AI systems make these judgments based on observable performance metrics (error rates, response times, task completion), which capture some but not all of the relevant information.

## 6.7 Research Evidence: AI-Assisted vs. AI-Dependent Output

### 6.7.1 Writing Studies

The empirical literature on AI-assisted L2 writing is growing rapidly but remains methodologically uneven. Several patterns have emerged from studies conducted between 2023 and 2026:

**Short-term accuracy gains.** Studies consistently find that L2 writers using AI tools produce texts with fewer grammatical and lexical errors than writers without AI support (Li et al., 2015; Koltovskaia, 2020; O'Regan et al., 2020). This is unsurprising: the AI's primary function is error correction, and it performs this function effectively.

**No transfer to unassisted performance.** The more troubling finding is that these accuracy gains rarely transfer to subsequent unassisted writing. Koltovskaia (2020), Yan (2023), and Kim and Kim (2024) all found that learners who used AI writing tools extensively did not show greater improvement on unassisted writing tasks compared to control groups. The accuracy gains were tool-dependent rather than learner-internal.

**Reduced revision depth.** Studies of the writing process have found that AI-assisted writers engage in less substantive revision. Escalante et al. (2023) found that students using AI spent less time on planning and formulation and more time on surface-level editing. Warschauer (2024) found that students using AI writing tools made fewer content-level revisions (adding, deleting, or reorganizing ideas) and more surface-level revisions (correcting grammar and spelling), suggesting that the AI directed attention toward accuracy at the expense of higher-order writing processes.

**Affective benefits.** Learners consistently report positive attitudes toward AI writing tools, citing reduced anxiety, increased confidence, and the perception of improved writing quality (Kohnke et al., 2023). These affective benefits are real and should not be dismissed, but they must be weighed against the evidence that the tools may not promote long-term development of independent writing ability.

### 6.7.2 Speaking Studies

Research on AI-assisted speaking practice and output is less extensive than the writing research but is growing. Studies have focused primarily on pronunciation (covered in detail in Chapter 8) and conversational fluency:

**Fluency gains.** Tai and Chen (2023) found that students practicing speaking with an AI tutor showed gains in speech rate and mean length of utterance over an eight-week period. Huang and Song (2024) found similar fluency gains among Chinese EFL learners using a voice-enabled AI conversation partner. These gains appear to reflect the benefit of increased practice opportunities rather than anything specific to AI interaction.

**Limited complexity and accuracy gains.** Speaking studies have generally not found significant improvements in syntactic complexity or grammatical accuracy from AI speaking practice (Han & Finkelstein, 2024). This may reflect the nature of AI conversation: because AI chatbots tend to accommodate the learner's level rather than pushing them to produce more complex language, the conditions for pushed output are not created.

**Pronunciation improvement.** As noted in Section 6.4.2, studies of ASR-based pronunciation tools have shown positive results for targeted segmental features but limited evidence for suprasegmental improvement. The binary nature of ASR feedback (recognized/not recognized) may not provide the nuanced information learners need to improve their overall pronunciation quality.

### 6.7.3 Comparative Studies: AI-Assisted vs. Human-Mediated Output

The most informative studies are those that directly compare AI-mediated and human-mediated production practice. The emerging pattern from these comparative studies suggests a division of labor:

**AI advantages:**
- Greater quantity of practice (available 24/7, infinitely patient)
- More consistent error identification (does not miss errors due to fatigue or inattention)
- Lower anxiety (no social judgment)
- Detailed metalinguistic explanations on demand

**Human advantages:**
- Genuine communicative pressure (real interlocutor with real comprehension needs)
- Dynamic, contingent scaffolding within the ZPD
- Pushed output through interactional demands (clarification requests, follow-up questions)
- Pragmatic modeling and sociolinguistic feedback
- Social motivation and accountability

These findings align with the complementarity principle proposed in Chapter 5: AI and human-mediated production practice serve different functions, and the most effective programs are likely those that integrate both strategically.

### 6.7.4 Longitudinal Evidence

The most critical gap in the current evidence base is the absence of longitudinal studies tracking learner development over extended periods of AI-assisted versus AI-independent production. Most existing studies span 8-16 weeks, which may be insufficient to detect the effects of AI scaffolding (or AI dependency) on long-term productive competence. The few longer-term studies that exist (Yan, 2023; Escalante et al., 2023) suggest that the risks of AI dependency increase over time, as learners habituate to AI support and increasingly outsource cognitive processes that they could perform independently. But these studies are small in scale and limited in scope.

The field urgently needs large-scale, longitudinal studies that track learner writing and speaking development over semesters or years, comparing different levels of AI integration, measuring both AI-assisted and unassisted performance at multiple time points, and examining the cognitive processes (through think-aloud protocols, keystroke logging, or eye-tracking) that learners engage in when producing language with and without AI support.

## 6.8 Pedagogical Implications: Designing AI-Mediated Production Tasks

### 6.8.1 Principles for Task Design

Based on the theoretical analysis and empirical evidence presented in this chapter, the following principles should guide the design of AI-mediated production tasks:

**1. Preserve the production process.** AI should support learners in producing language, not produce language for them. This means designing tasks that keep the learner in the formulator's seat -- making lexical choices, building syntactic frames, applying morphological rules -- even when AI is available to assist.

**2. Maintain desirable difficulty.** AI scaffolding should reduce frustration without eliminating challenge. The ideal AI writing or speaking tool identifies errors but does not correct them, provides hints but not answers, and asks questions that guide the learner's thinking without doing the thinking for them.

**3. Require independent production.** Every AI-mediated production task should include a component that requires the learner to produce language independently, without AI support. This ensures that the cognitive processes identified by Swain, DeKeyser, and Levelt are engaged at least some of the time.

**4. Make the learning process visible.** AI tools should be designed to make the learner's production process -- their errors, their revisions, their decisions -- visible and available for reflection. Keystroke logging, draft comparison, and error tracking can help learners and teachers understand how AI is affecting the production process.

**5. Graduate the scaffolding.** AI support should decrease as proficiency increases. Beginning learners may legitimately need more AI assistance; advanced learners should be producing independently with AI used only for targeted checking.

### 6.8.2 Task Types

Several task types effectively leverage AI while preserving the productive functions of output:

**AI as interlocutor in information-gap tasks.** The learner must communicate specific information to the AI, which is programmed not to fill in gaps or accommodate errors. The learner must produce clear, accurate output to accomplish the communicative goal.

**AI as editor, not author.** The learner writes independently, then submits the text to an AI for feedback. The AI identifies errors and asks questions ("What tense did you intend here?" "Is this the word you mean?") but does not provide corrections. The learner must produce the corrections independently.

**Dictogloss with AI support.** The learner listens to a passage and reconstructs it from memory, then compares their reconstruction with the AI's reconstruction. The comparison highlights gaps in the learner's productive knowledge without providing the correct forms in advance.

**Collaborative writing with AI as a participant.** The learner and the AI take turns writing sentences in a story, with the AI's contributions at the learner's level. The learner must match the AI's quality in their own turns, creating push without eliminating production.

**Translation post-editing.** The learner receives an MT translation and must identify and correct errors, requiring the application of metalinguistic knowledge without the burden of initial text generation. This is particularly useful for advanced learners who can evaluate MT output critically.

**Progressive disclosure.** For speaking tasks, the AI provides a complex scenario but reveals information gradually, requiring the learner to adapt their output to new information in real time. This creates the communicative pressure for pushed output without the social anxiety of human interaction.

### 6.8.3 Assessment Considerations

The availability of AI production tools has profound implications for language assessment. If learners can use AI to produce near-native quality writing, traditional written assessments that allow unrestricted AI access cannot meaningfully evaluate productive competence. Assessment designs must account for the AI variable:

**Proctored production assessments.** In-class or proctored assessments in which AI tools are unavailable provide the clearest measure of independent productive competence. These assessments should not be viewed as punitive but as informative: they show the learner and the teacher what the learner can do independently, which is the competence that matters when AI is not available.

**Process-focused assessment.** Assessing the writing or speaking process -- through keystroke logging, draft analysis, or recording of revision decisions -- rather than the final product can reveal whether the learner is engaging in the cognitive processes that support development, regardless of whether AI is used.

**Portfolio assessment with AI-use documentation.** Requiring learners to document their AI use (what tools they used, what suggestions they accepted or rejected, what they produced independently) provides evidence of the learner's engagement with AI as a learning tool rather than a ghostwriter.

## 6.9 The Dependency Problem: A Framework for Analysis

### 6.9.1 Defining AI Dependency

AI dependency in language production can be defined as a state in which the learner's productive performance degrades significantly when AI support is removed. A learner who writes competently with Grammarly but produces error-filled text without it has become dependent on the tool for accuracy monitoring. A learner who converses fluently with ChatGPT's help but cannot sustain a conversation independently has become dependent on the AI for formulation support.

Dependency is not inherently negative -- we are all "dependent" on tools in some sense (writers depend on word processors, speakers depend on gesture and facial expression). The concern in language learning is that dependency on AI may substitute for the development of internal competence that the learner needs for independent functioning. A writer who depends on spell-check for occasional typos has a trivial dependency; a writer who depends on AI for every sentence has outsourced the writing process itself.

### 6.9.2 Risk Factors for AI Dependency

Several factors may increase the risk of AI dependency in language learners:

**Proficiency level.** Lower-proficiency learners, who have the least internal linguistic resources to draw on, may be most vulnerable to dependency because the AI fills a large gap between what they want to say and what they can say independently. Paradoxically, these are also the learners for whom productive practice is most important.

**Motivation orientation.** Learners with performance goals (wanting to produce correct output) may be more susceptible to dependency than learners with learning goals (wanting to develop competence), because AI provides an efficient path to performance without the effort of learning (Dweck, 1986). The distinction between performance and mastery orientations has direct implications for how learners approach AI tools.

**Institutional incentives.** When assessment focuses on the quality of the final product rather than the learning process, learners have rational incentives to use AI to maximize product quality, regardless of the effect on their development. Institutional assessment practices can thus either mitigate or exacerbate the dependency risk.

**Tool design.** AI tools that correct automatically and silently (e.g., autocorrect) produce more dependency than tools that flag errors and require learner action (e.g., spell-check with wavy underlines). The design choices made by AI tool developers directly affect the likelihood of dependency.

### 6.9.3 Mitigating Dependency

The strategies for mitigating AI dependency follow from the principles articulated throughout this chapter:

1. **Regular unassisted production.** Learners should regularly produce language without AI support, to maintain and develop independent competence.
2. **Metacognitive awareness.** Learners should be taught to monitor their own AI use and to recognize when they are relying on AI for processes they should be performing themselves.
3. **Progressive withdrawal.** AI scaffolding should be systematically reduced over time, following the developmental trajectory of the learner's proficiency.
4. **Process over product.** Assessment and feedback should prioritize the learning process (effort, strategy use, revision quality) over the final product (accuracy, fluency, complexity of the submitted text).
5. **Teacher mediation.** The teacher should monitor learners' AI use patterns, intervene when dependency is developing, and guide learners toward more productive modes of AI integration.

## 6.10 Conclusion

The relationship between AI and learner output is the most consequential issue in the current landscape of AI-assisted language learning. If output serves the cognitive functions that Swain theorized -- noticing, hypothesis testing, and metalinguistic reflection -- then tools that perform output on the learner's behalf are not merely providing assistance; they are removing a mechanism of acquisition. The paradox is real, and its implications are significant: the same AI capabilities that make language production easier for learners may make language *acquisition* harder.

This chapter has argued that the paradox is not irresolvable. AI tools can be designed, integrated, and pedagogically managed in ways that support productive struggle rather than eliminating it. The key is to maintain the learner in the role of producer -- formulating, testing, revising, and producing language -- while using AI to modulate the difficulty of that production so that it falls within the zone of desirable difficulty. This requires deliberate task design, graduated scaffolding, regular unassisted practice, and assessment systems that evaluate independent competence rather than AI-mediated performance.

The evidence base for these principles is still developing. The studies reviewed in this chapter suggest that AI writing tools improve the quality of assisted performance but do not reliably improve the quality of unassisted performance -- a finding that should give pause to both enthusiasts and practitioners. The field needs longitudinal, large-scale, process-focused research that examines how learners develop productive competence over time under different conditions of AI support. Until that evidence is available, the prudent approach is to treat AI as a supplement to, not a replacement for, the effortful, sometimes frustrating, but ultimately essential process of producing language oneself.

The next chapter examines corrective feedback -- the mechanism through which errors in output are signaled and potentially repaired. If this chapter has focused on the production of language, Chapter 7 focuses on what happens when that production goes wrong and on the role of AI as the "tireless interlocutor" who never misses an error but may not always provide feedback in the most acquisitionally effective form.
